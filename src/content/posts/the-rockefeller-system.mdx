## PART II: THE MIND — CENTRALIZING AMERICAN EDUCATION

## The Prussian Model Comes to America

The modern American school did not arise from American soil. It was copied, nearly feature for feature, from the 18th– and 19th-century Prussian state-school system — a system explicitly designed by Frederick the Great and his successors to produce obedient soldiers, compliant civil servants, and factory workers who could follow orders. The age-grading system, the bells between periods, the rows of desks, the standardized testing, the separation of children from their parents for most of the day — all of it came from Prussia.

Horace Mann, appointed Secretary of the Massachusetts Board of Education in 1837, traveled to Prussia and returned convinced America should adopt the same model. His reports became the foundation of compulsory public schooling in the United States. But the system did not scale nationally until Rockefeller money arrived.

## The General Education Board (1903)

Rockefeller established the General Education Board with an initial endowment of $1 million, later expanded to over $180 million by the 1920s — equivalent to several billion in today's currency. The Board's stated purpose was "the promotion of education within the United States of America, without distinction of race, sex, or creed." The real purpose was documented by Rockefeller's own chief education advisor.

## Frederick T. Gates — The Quiet Blueprint

Frederick Taylor Gates was a Baptist minister who became the principal architect of Rockefeller's philanthropy. In *Occasional Letter No. 1*, published by the General Education Board in 1906, Gates wrote what amounts to a confession of intent. He described the rural population as raw material to be shaped, and declared that the new schools would not produce philosophers, scholars, or men of letters. They would produce, instead, a population tractable enough to be directed by their superiors.

*The task is to shape the child, not to educate him.*

This is the Prussian-industrial school exported, scaled, and endowed. The one-room schoolhouse, which had been a local, community-accountable institution where the teacher was often hired directly by the parents, was systematically replaced with a centralized, consolidated, bureaucratically managed system. By the 1930s over 130,000 one-room schools existed in America; by the 1970s, fewer than 1,000 remained. This was not progress. This was consolidation.

## The Carnegie Connection

Andrew Carnegie's foundations ran parallel to Rockefeller's. The Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching defined what a "credit hour" was, what qualified as a "high school," and what counted as a "college." The Carnegie Unit — still the basis of American high-school graduation requirements — was invented in 1906 to standardize the output of American schools. Carnegie and Rockefeller coordinated closely; when the Carnegie Foundation needed to reshape American medicine a few years later, Rockefeller's money followed immediately after.

In 1908 the Carnegie Foundation commissioned Abraham Flexner to evaluate every medical school in the United States and Canada. Flexner was not a physician. Before the Flexner Report, American medicine was pluralistic. Patients could choose. After Flexner, only the allopathic schools received foundation money, accreditation, and state licensing recognition. Within two decades, roughly half of America's medical schools were closed. The majority of those closed were homeopathic, eclectic, or serving women and minorities.

## PART IV: THE TABLE — CENTRALIZING GLOBAL FOOD

In 1943 the Rockefeller Foundation partnered with the Mexican government to launch the Mexican Agricultural Program, headed eventually by Norman Borlaug. The stated goal was to increase wheat and corn yields in Mexico. The actual effect was the creation of a new global agricultural model dependent on three things no traditional farmer had ever needed: proprietary hybrid seed, synthetic petroleum-based fertilizer, and chemical pesticides.

Borlaug received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970 and was celebrated as the man who "saved a billion lives." By 1961 the Ford Foundation had joined Rockefeller to export the model to the Philippines, India, Pakistan, and across Asia and Africa. Over fifty years the Rockefeller Foundation invested approximately $600 million directly in the Green Revolution. The narrative was humanitarian. The structural outcome was total dependence of the world's food supply on petroleum-derived inputs — the same petroleum empire Rockefeller had built.

**Hybrid seed.** Traditional farmers saved seed year to year — Yahuah's design built into the grain itself. Hybrid seed does not breed true; the farmer must buy new seed every season from the supplier. One generation of adoption, and the ability to save seed is gone.

**Pesticides and herbicides.** The monoculture of hybrid varieties is genetically uniform and therefore catastrophically vulnerable to pests and disease. The only way to keep the system standing is continuous chemical application — another petroleum product.

Every link in the chain runs back to Standard Oil. The seed is patented, the fertilizer is petroleum, the pesticide is petroleum, the tractor burns petroleum, and the shipping container burns petroleum. The Green Revolution did not feed the world.

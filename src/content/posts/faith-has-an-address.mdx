## The Sentence Everyone Finishes Wrong

Walk into almost any church and you will hear the same instruction, phrased a dozen ways: "Believe in Him." "Just have faith in Christ." "Put your trust in Jesus." It is repeated so often that no one stops to examine it. And on the surface there is nothing wrong with the words. The Bible does say to believe on the Son. This study is not going to deny a single verse that says so.

But a sentence can be made of true words and still point the wrong direction. That is exactly what has happened here. In the mouth of the average Christian, "have faith in Christ" has quietly become the whole target — the place where trust starts, aims, and stops. The Son has been turned into the final destination of belief. And the Father — the One who made the promise, the One being trusted in every faith story in the Bible — has been moved to the background, or dropped out of the picture altogether.

This is not a small drift. Faith is not measured by how strongly a person feels it. It is measured by what it is aimed at. An arrow is only as good as its target. A person can believe with all their strength and still be believing in the wrong direction. So the question that matters is not "Do you have faith?" The question is: "Where is it pointed, and where does it come to rest?"

Scripture answers that question the same way every time, from Genesis to Revelation. And the answer is not the answer most Christians would give. This study will prove it wrong from six different directions — the definition of faith, the words of the very verses used to defend it, the direction every believing text actually runs, the reason the Son can be trusted at all, the fruit that faith leaves behind, and finally the terrible cost of aiming the arrow at the wrong place. By the end, the sentence will be finished correctly.

## Angle One — Faith Is Defined by Its Target

Start where the Bible starts. When Scripture gives its one great definition of what faith is, it does not describe a feeling. It describes an aim. And it names the target out loud:

**Hebrews 11:6**
> But without faith it is impossible to please him: for he that cometh to God must believe that he is, and that he is a rewarder of them that diligently seek him.

Read what the target is. The one who comes must believe that HE IS — that Yahuah exists — and that HE IS A REWARDER. The whole aim of faith is set on Yahuah and on His character as a keeper of promises. This is the verse that opens the Bible's hall of faith, and it points the arrow at the Father before a single hero is named.

And this is no name pulled from a hat. Each phrase of the verse is drawn straight from the way the Old Testament speaks of Yahuah — and of Him alone. "He that cometh to God" is the One the Psalms call near to all who seek Him: "The LORD (Yahuah) is nigh unto all them that call upon him" (Psalm 145:18). "Believe that he is" reaches back to Isaiah, where Yahuah ties believing directly to His own self-existence — "believe me, and understand that I am he: before me there was no God formed, neither shall there be after me" (Isaiah 43:10) — and the very name Yahuah is built from the Hebrew "to be," so "he is" is simply who He is. And "a rewarder of them that diligently seek him" is the promise Yahuah made to Abram: "I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward" (Genesis 15:1) — spoken four verses before Abram believed Yahuah and it was counted to him for righteousness, the pattern the whole chapter is built on.

So the target is fixed and named before the first hero appears: the self-existent Yahuah who is near, who simply IS, and who rewards the seeker. And a Son who was sent, given, and raised cannot be the "he is" who had none before Him — the arrow cannot slide back to the Son.

Then look at who fills that chapter. Every one of them is trusting something Yahuah said. By faith Abel obeyed Yahuah's word about sacrifice. By faith Noah acted on a warning from Yahuah about things not yet seen. By faith Abraham left home because Yahuah told him to, and believed a promise of a son when his body was as good as dead. Sarah "judged him faithful who had promised" (Hebrews 11:11). Not one of them is described as trusting a second person alongside Yahuah. Every arrow in the chapter is aimed at the Father and at His word.

Abraham is the pattern Paul returns to again and again, and Paul is careful to say exactly where Abraham's faith was aimed:

**Romans 4:20–21**
> He staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God; And being fully persuaded that, what he had promised, he was able also to perform.

Strong in faith — aimed at the promise of Yahuah, fully persuaded that the One who promised could perform. That is the definition in action. Faith is not a warm cloud in the chest. It is the soul leaning its full weight on what the Father has said. Change the target and you do not have a stronger faith or a weaker faith. You have a different religion. And the average Christian has quietly changed the target.

## Angle Two — The Word They Quietly Changed

Here is where the ground gives way beneath the popular teaching. The verses most often quoted to prove "faith IN Christ" do not actually say that in the original language. They say "faith OF Christ." And the King James translators, working straight from the Greek, preserved it honestly.

**πίστις Χριστου** *(pistis Christou)* — the faith OF Christ — a possessive form; the faithfulness that belongs to Him

Look at the King James wording in the very verses that built the modern slogan:

**Romans 3:22**
> Even the righteousness of God which is by faith of Jesus Christ unto all and upon all them that believe.

**Galatians 2:20**
> … the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God, who loved me, and gave himself for me.

**Philippians 3:9**
> … not having mine own righteousness … but that which is through the faith of Christ, the righteousness which is of God by faith.

Faith OF Christ. Not faith aimed at Christ, but the faithfulness that Christ Himself carried — His own trust, His own obedience, His own leaning on the Father all the way to the tree. That is the ground a believer stands on: not the strength of their own believing, but the proven faithfulness of the Messiah. Modern translations quietly changed the little word "of" into "in," and with one swapped letter they moved the whole weight off the faithfulness OF the Son and onto the mental act of the believer pointed AT the Son.

Watch how carefully Paul actually speaks when both ideas appear in the same breath:

**Galatians 2:16**
> Knowing that a man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ, even we have believed in Jesus Christ, that we might be justified by the faith of Christ.

Two different things sit side by side. There is "the faith OF Jesus Christ" — the faith He Himself possessed — and there is "we have believed" — our response to it. Paul never collapses the two into a slogan. The average Christian has collapsed them, kept only the second half, and aimed it at the Son as if it were the whole of salvation. The verse they quote to prove it is the very verse that pulls the two apart.

And once the two are pulled apart, Galatians 2:16 becomes the single strongest verse in this entire study. A man is justified "by the faith OF Jesus Christ" — by the faith Christ Himself carried. What faith did He carry? Faith in the Father. Yahushua trusted Yahuah perfectly, leaned His whole life on the Father's word, and did not waver from the wilderness to the tree. That faith — His faith — is the faith that justifies. And the law cannot manufacture it: "by the works of the law shall no flesh be justified." No amount of rule-keeping can produce the faith of Christ in a person. It can only be received and shared.

So to be justified is to come to carry the same faith Yahushua carried — His faith in the Father — not a faith aimed AT Him, but the faith that was IN Him, now living in us. This is why the direction of faith is not a side issue; it is the whole issue. The justifying faith is itself faith in the Father, because it is Christ's own faith, and Christ's faith never rested on Himself. It rested on Yahuah. A faith that stops at the Son cannot be the faith OF the Son — because the Son's faith did not stop at Himself.

The most quoted verse in all of Christianity says the very same thing, once it is read slowly:

**John 3:16**
> For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have everlasting life.

Believe in whom? In the Son — but notice how the verse names Him: the One the Father GAVE, the promise Yahuah sent into the world. To believe in Him is to believe the message the Father sent. And the person who truly believes that promise does not simply admire the Son from a distance; he receives the Son's own faith and comes to trust the Father the way the Son trusted Him — and so he does not perish. Believing the promise Yahuah sent, and coming to share the faith of the One He sent, are one and the same motion. That is how a person is justified: not by the law, but by carrying the faith of Christ toward the Father.

## Angle Three — Every "Believe on the Son" Text Runs Through Him to the Father

Now to the honest part. There are verses that plainly say to believe on the Son. "Believe on the Lord Jesus Christ, and thou shalt be saved" (Acts 16:31). "God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in him should not perish" (John 3:16). This study will not pretend those verses are not there. The point is not that they are wrong. The point is the direction they run — and every one of them runs through the Son and comes to rest on the Father.

Start with the words of Yahushua Himself, when men asked what Yahuah required of them:

**John 6:29**
> This is the work of God, that ye believe on him whom he hath sent.

Believe on him whom He hath sent. The belief is aimed at the Son, yes — but the Son is named here as the Sent One, and belief in the Sent One is belief that reaches back to the One who sent Him. You do not trust a messenger by forgetting who the message is from. John says the same thing even more plainly:

**1 John 5:10–11**
> He that believeth not God hath made him a liar; because he believeth not the record that God gave of his Son. And this is the record, that God hath given to us eternal life, and this life is in his Son.

The Son is the record — the testimony Yahuah gave. To refuse the Son is to call the Father a liar. So believing on the Son is nothing other than believing the Father's testimony. The two can never be separated. But watch where the weight finally settles, because Peter states it in words that leave no room to argue:

**1 Peter 1:21**
> Who by him do believe in God, that raised him up from the dead, and gave him glory; that your faith and hope might be in God.

By him — through the Son — we believe in God, so that our faith and hope "might be in God." There is the arrow, drawn in full: it passes through the Son and lands in the Father. Paul aims it the same way, describing saving belief not as trust in Jesus but as trust in the One who raised Him:

**Romans 4:24**
> … if we believe on him that raised up Jesus our Lord from the dead.

Even the famous line in John 14 keeps the order straight. "Ye believe in God, believe also in me" (John 14:1). Notice: God first. The Son is not offered as a replacement target but as the way to the One already believed in — which He states outright three verses later: "No man cometh unto the Father, but by me" (John 14:6). By me — to the Father. The Son is the road; the Father is the One the road was built to reach.

And if any doubt remains about where Yahushua wanted faith aimed, He removed it in three words:

**Mark 11:22**
> And Jesus answering saith unto them, Have faith in God.

The Son Himself, given the perfect chance to say "have faith in me," said instead: have faith in God. That is the whole correction in the mouth of the Messiah.

## Angle Four — Why the Son Can Be Trusted: He Is the Promise

At this point a believing friend will object, and the objection is fair: "If faith rests on the Father, why does the Bible tie it so tightly to the Son?" The answer is the most important part of this study, and it is where the Son is lifted to His true and towering place — not lowered.

The Son can be trusted because the Son IS the promise. Yahuah did not make a scattered pile of unrelated promises. He made one central promise in the garden, and every other promise grows out of it:

**Genesis 3:15**
> … it shall bruise thy head, and thou shalt bruise his heel.

A Seed is coming who will crush the serpent. That is the first promise, and it is a person. Abraham was promised that Seed; David was promised that Seed on his throne. And Paul tells us plainly who the Seed always was:

**Galatians 3:16**
> … And to thy seed, which is Christ.

So the Son is not one promise sitting on a shelf beside the others. He is THE promise — the one Yahuah spoke first, the one every later promise depends on. Take Him away and there is no promise of righteousness, no promise of the Spirit, no inheritance left standing. He is the promise that made the other promises possible. That is why Paul can gather every promise Yahuah ever made into one place:

**2 Corinthians 1:20**
> For all the promises of God in him are yea, and in him Amen, unto the glory of God by us.

Every promise of the Father is confirmed — made "yea and Amen" — in the Son. He came, Paul says, "to confirm the promises made unto the fathers" (Romans 15:8). So now the friend's sentence can be finished correctly. "Believe in the Messiah" is true — and here is what it means: trust the promise Yahuah made and kept. To believe the Son IS to believe the Father's promise, because the Son is that promise delivered. You are never choosing between trusting the Father and trusting the Son. But the ground you rest on — the One who cannot lie, the One who promised — is Yahuah. The Son is not sold short by this. He is exalted by it: not a rival to the Father, but the living Yes to everything the Father ever said.

## Angle Five — Even the Fruit Points Home

The average teaching makes one more wrong turn. Having aimed faith at the Son, it then measures that faith by the works a person can rack up — a scoreboard of religious achievement held up as proof. But the Bible never calls the evidence of faith "works a person produces." It calls it fruit. And fruit is grown, not manufactured.

**Galatians 5:22–23**
> But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, longsuffering, gentleness, goodness, faith, meekness, temperance.

Notice whose fruit it is: the fruit of the Spirit — and the Spirit is Yahuah's. A branch does not strain to make grapes. It stays joined to the vine and the life of the vine pushes the fruit out through it. Yahushua said it exactly: "I am the vine, ye are the branches… without me ye can do nothing" (John 15:5). And who owns and tends the vineyard? "My Father is the husbandman" (John 15:1).

Read the grammar and it settles the matter: "the fruit OF the Spirit," not the fruit of the believer. And Yahushua leaves no wiggle room — "without me ye can do nothing" means the branch produces exactly none of it on its own. Not less. Not lower quality. None. The branch does not get credit for the grape any more than an irrigation pipe gets praised for the harvest. It only carries what the vine draws up from the root.

And here is the thread that ties this angle to all the others: the fruit does not only come FROM the Father — it is aimed back AT Him. Paul says we are joined to the risen Messiah "that we should bring forth fruit unto God" (Romans 7:4). He prays that believers be "filled with the fruits of righteousness… unto the glory and praise of God" (Philippians 1:11). Yahushua says it plainly: "Herein is my Father glorified, that ye bear much fruit" (John 15:8). The fruit comes from the Father, flows through the vine, and returns to the Father's glory. It is the same arrow one more time. A believer's obedience does not terminate on the Son, and it certainly does not terminate on the believer. It points home to Yahuah.

So the fruit was never proof of our own strength, and it was never meant to be. It is His righteousness carried by a connected branch and borne back to His glory. A person is not received by producing it, but by the lean — and even the fruit that follows keeps the arrow aimed exactly where faith was aimed all along. The target never changes.

## Angle Six — What the Misfire Costs, and How to Re-Aim

This is not a debate over a technicality. When the arrow of faith is stopped at the Son and never allowed to reach the Father, something terrible happens to the whole picture of Yahuah — and it is the exact opposite of the truth.

In the popular telling, the Father becomes the angry judge in the background and the Son becomes the loving one who steps in to rescue us from Him. But turn to the most quoted verse in Christianity and read who is doing the loving and the giving:

**John 3:16**
> For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son …

The Father is the One who loved. The Father is the One who gave. The Son is the gift, not the reluctant judge He was sent to talk down. The redeemer who paid the price is Yahuah, and the price He paid was His own Son. Aim faith only at the Son and this entire truth flips upside down: the Giver is mistaken for the threat, and the Gift is mistaken for the source. The gospel is inverted while the words stay the same.

Re-aim the arrow and everything stands upright again. There is one Yahuah, and Scripture tells us plainly who He is:

**1 Corinthians 8:6**
> But to us there is but one God, the Father, of whom are all things, and we in him; and one Lord Jesus Christ, by whom are all things, and we by him.

One God — the Father. One Lord — Yahushua, the one through whom all things come to us and through whom we come to the Father. Paul does not aim worship-trust at two. He names the Father as the one God and the Son as the Lord through whom we reach Him. This is simply the Shema, unbroken: "Hear, O Israel: Yahuah our God is one Yahuah" (Deuteronomy 6:4).

So finish the sentence the way the Bible finishes it. "Believe in Him" — through the Son. "Have faith in Christ" — because He is the promise Yahuah made and kept. But do not stop the arrow there. Let it fly all the way home, and rest your whole weight where every hero of faith rested theirs: on the Father who promised, who cannot lie, and who has never once let the weight fall. That is not a lesser faith in the Son. It is the only faith that honors why the Son came at all.

> The arrow of faith passes through the Son. It comes to rest on the Father. A church that stops the arrow at the Son has trusted the way and forgotten the One the way leads to.

> "And Jesus answering saith unto them, Have faith in God." — Mark 11:22

*Who Shall Ascend the Hill?*

•  •  •

A pastor stood up and told his congregation that no one has clean hands and a pure heart. No one. It was offered as comfort — none of us measure up, so don’t feel bad. But it wasn’t comfort. It was a quiet surrender. And it flatly contradicts the very Psalm the phrase is pulled from.

**Psalm 24:3–4**

*Who shall ascend into the hill of Yahuah? or who shall stand in his holy place? He that hath clean hands, and a pure heart; who hath not lifted up his soul unto vanity, nor sworn deceitfully.*

Yahuah asks who may ascend His hill — and then He answers His own question. He does not say “no one.” He describes a person. Clean hands. A pure heart. If it were impossible, the answer would be a shut door. Instead it is a doorway with a description written over it.

**Clean Hands — נְקִי כַפַּיִם (neqi kappayim)**

The word for *clean* is *naqi* (H5355), from the root *naqah* (H5352). *Naqah* means to be clean, to be free, to be acquitted, to be held innocent, to go unpunished. Underneath it carries the picture of being *emptied out* — poured clean. The hand is not scrubbed on the surface; it is emptied of what stains it.

And *kaph* (H3709) is not the arm or the clenched fist. It is the open, cupped *palm* — the working hand, the hand that reaches out and *does*. So clean hands are clean **deeds**. This is not a mood. It is conduct: what the hand has done, and what it refuses to do.

*Naqi* is the same word used of the innocent whose blood must not be shed, and of the man cleared of a charge. It is verdict language. To have clean hands is to stand before Yahuah with your actions *acquitted* — not because sin was never there, but because it has been dealt with and abandoned.

**A Pure Heart — בַר לֵבָב (bar levav)**

*Pure* is *bar* (H1249), from the root *barar* (H1305). *Barar* is a farming word: to purge, to purify, to select, to polish — and most vividly, to **winnow**. Winnowing is when the threshed grain is thrown up into the air so the wind carries off the chaff and the clean grain falls back down. A pure heart is not a heart that never held chaff. It is a heart that has been *sifted* — the worthless blown away, the good kept.

*Levav* (H3824) is the inner man — the will, the mind, the seat of intent. Pair it with the hands and you have the whole person: the inside and the outside pointing one direction. The heart intends it; the hands do it. Nothing split. Nothing performed for show while the real affection sits somewhere else.

And this is where wholeness enters. A winnowed heart is an *undivided* heart — *shalem*, complete. When the heart is *shalem* toward Yahuah, *shalom* flows out of it: not the modern idea of a private calm feeling, but an outward fullness that moves from a whole heart into everything it touches. The pure heart and the whole heart are one heart seen from two angles. Divided loyalty is exactly the chaff that breaks the flow. Winnow it out, and the river runs clean again.

Verse 4 finishes the portrait: this person “hath not lifted up his soul unto vanity, nor sworn deceitfully.” He has not given his life to emptiness, and he does not speak falsely. Clean hands, pure heart, honest tongue — a *whole* man.

**“But We’re All Sinners”**

Here is the doctrine that pastor was leaning on: we are all sinners, no one is righteous, so no one can have clean hands. It is built on a real verse —

**Romans 3:23**

*For all have sinned, and come short of the glory of Yahuah.*

But look at the tense. All *have sinned*. Past. It describes the universal need that brought every one of us to the blood in the first place. It does not say all *keep on* sinning forever with no way out. It is the diagnosis, not a life sentence.

The modern church has turned “I am a sinner” into a permanent identity — “just a sinner, still failing every day, and that’s okay.” But Scripture does not leave the redeemed there. It calls them *out* of it. Look at what Yahushua actually said to the ones He forgave:

To the man at the pool: “**sin no more**, lest a worse thing come unto thee” (John 5:14).

To the woman they were ready to stone: “go, and **sin no more**” (John 8:11).

You cannot command the impossible. If sinlessness were truly beyond reach, “sin no more” would be cruelty. It is not a taunt. It is a real instruction to a person who *can* obey it.

And John — the same John the “we all sin” crowd quotes from 1 John 1:8 — keeps writing:

**1 John 3:6, 9**

*Whosoever abideth in him sinneth not... Whosoever is born of Elohim doth not commit sin... and he cannot sin, because he is born of Elohim.*

Three verses earlier, sin itself is defined: “sin is the **transgression of the law**” (1 John 3:4). So “we are all helpless sinners forever” is, underneath, a doctrine that excuses ongoing lawbreaking. That is why it preaches so comfortably — it lets the Torah stay buried.

But the new creation is real:

**2 Corinthians 5:17**

*If any man be in Messiah, he is a new creature: old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new.*

And to the new creature the command stands: “Be ye holy; for I am holy” (1 Peter 1:16, quoting Leviticus 11:44). “Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect” (Matthew 5:48). His commandments “are **not grievous**” (1 John 5:3). Sinless is not a fantasy the redeemed can never touch. It is the *calling* — and a calling, by definition, can be answered.

**Two Stands at the Hill**

The confusion clears the moment you see the two stages. *All have sinned* — that is settled at entry, by the blood. The hands are washed clean by atonement; the record is acquitted (*naqah*). But entry is not the end. The one who ascends the hill then *walks* — and the walk is the winnowed heart kept whole and the clean hands kept clean, the commandments of Yahuah lived out.

The pastor collapsed both stages into one word: *impossible*. The blood you cannot earn — true. But he then declared the *walk* impossible too, and quietly excused the disobedience. Scripture separates them. The blood brings you to the foot of the hill with a clean record. The undivided heart carries you up it.

**The Priest at the Laver**

And here the two stages open all the way up — because Psalm 24 was never asking an abstract question. “Who shall *ascend into the hill* of Yahuah? or who shall *stand in his holy place*?” That is sanctuary language: who may approach to serve. Clean hands and a pure heart is the answer to a *temple-access* question. The priesthood is not imported into this Psalm — it is native to it.

Watch how a priest was made. His consecration opened with **water**: “wash them with water” (Exodus 29:4; Leviticus 8:6). Before he could serve, the man was washed — brought from common to holy, a new man made for the sanctuary. There is the new creation. Then the **blood** was put on the hand — the right thumb (Leviticus 8:23–24). At the very moment of ordination, the priest’s hand was marked with blood. So “clean hands” is not only conduct; it is the *consecrated* hand — washed and blood-marked, admitted to serve.

But the priest was not finished at ordination. He came back to the laver every time he served: “wash their hands and their feet, **that they die not**” (Exodus 30:19–21). Ongoing purification, or he could not stand in the holy place. That is the pure heart — the *barar* winnowing that never stops, the walk kept clean day after day.

Ezekiel joins the whole chain in one breath:

**Ezekiel 36:25–27**

*I will sprinkle clean water upon you, and ye shall be clean... A new heart also will I give you... and cause you to walk in my statutes.*

Clean water, new heart, walk in the commandments — the exact order, spoken to the covenant people long before the cross.

And this priesthood is *yours*. “Ye also... are built up a spiritual house, an holy priesthood” (1 Peter 2:5). “A royal priesthood” (2:9). “Ye are the temple of the living Elohim” (2 Corinthians 6:16). “Made us kings and priests” (Revelation 1:6). You are the temple; you are the priest. Psalm 24 is asking whether *you* may enter *yourself* — washed to be made new, blood on the hand to be admitted, and back to the laver again and again to keep the walk clean enough to serve.

**You Can Ascend**

So when a preacher says no one has clean hands and a pure heart, answer him with the Psalm he is quoting. Yahuah asked the question and Yahuah gave the answer, and the answer was not “no one.” It was a man with emptied hands, a sifted heart, a life not spent on vanity, and a tongue that does not lie.

That man exists. You are called to *be* him — not by pretending the chaff was never there, but by letting it be winnowed out, keeping the hands clean, and walking up the hill Yahuah built for exactly this kind of person to climb.

**Isaiah 9:6**

**The Names the Child Would Bear**

*A throne-name that proclaims the Father, laid on the Son*

A name can ring with Yahuah and still belong to the one He sent.

--- The Standing Stone ---

> **Behind "LORD" in your Bible lies a hidden name --- in the Hebrew it
> is Yahuah** *Psalm 83:18***; Yahuah is the Father** *Isaiah 63:16***;
> Yahuah is the only God, beside Him there is no other** *Isaiah
> 45:5***; therefore Yahuah the Father is the only true God, leaving no
> room for a second or third person** *1 Corinthians 8:6***.**

Part One

**Framing the Problem**

***1.1 --- The Claim***

Defenders of a triune God point to *Isaiah 9:6* as one of their
strongest Old Testament proofs that Yahushua (Jesus) is Yahuah in the
flesh. The verse, as it appears in most English Bibles, seems to call a
child "Mighty God" and "Everlasting Father." On the surface, those
titles sound like clear proof of deity.

But there is a problem --- several, in fact. This is one of the most
varied verses in the entire Old Testament. The renderings are all over
the map. One translation reads it one way; another reads it another. The
ancient Greek translation reads it differently still. And the original
Hebrew disagrees with almost every modern English Bible. Something is
wrong here, and it is worth slowing down to see what.

***1.2 --- The Internal Contradiction***

Before we even open the Hebrew, notice this: the doctrine itself teaches
that the Son is not the Father --- they are held to be two separate
persons within the Godhead. But *Isaiah 9:6* calls this child
"Everlasting Father." If the child is Yahushua, and the titles belong to
him, then Yahushua is the Father. Yet the doctrine insists he is not. It
cannot have the verse both ways.

That alone should raise a flag. Either the titles are not about the
child at all, or the doctrine contradicts itself at this very verse. As
we will see, the Hebrew resolves the problem in a way most English
readers have never been shown.

***1.3 --- Hebrews 1:1--2 and the Son's Role***

*Hebrews 1:1-2* tells us that Yahuah spoke through the prophets in time
past, and "in these last days" has spoken through His Son. The Son was
therefore not the one speaking in Isaiah's day. Isaiah was hearing from
Yahuah directly. The child of *Isaiah 9:6* is being described by Yahuah
--- not by the Son speaking about himself.

Part Two

**Reading the Passage in Context**

***2.1 --- The Story Behind Isaiah 7--9***

You cannot understand *Isaiah 9:6* by reading it alone. Chapters 7
through 9 are one connected story. King Ahaz ruled the southern kingdom
--- Judah, with its capital at Jerusalem. To the north, two nations,
Syria (capital Damascus) and Israel/Ephraim (capital Samaria), had
formed an alliance to topple Ahaz and install their own puppet king
*Isaiah 7:1-6*. On top of that, the Assyrian army was sweeping through
the region, leveling everything in its path.

Yahuah sent Isaiah to Ahaz with a message: do not be afraid; Yahuah will
handle this. To seal it, Isaiah gave Ahaz a series of signs --- and each
sign was a child whose name carried a prophetic message.

***2.2 --- The Sign-Children of Isaiah***

*Isaiah 7:14* --- Immanuel. A young woman would bear a child named
"Immanuel," meaning "God is with us." It was a sign that Yahuah would
guard Judah. No one claims the child literally is God; the name
describes what Yahuah is doing --- being present with His people.

*Isaiah 8:1-4* --- Maher-Shalal-Hash-Baz. Isaiah's own son carried this
name, meaning "the spoil speeds, the prey hastens." It signaled that
Damascus and Samaria would fall to Assyria before the boy could even
talk. Again, no one claims the child is the spoil. The name describes
what Yahuah is about to do.

*Isaiah 9:6* --- The Prince of Peace. The same pattern continues. A
child is born, and his name carries a prophetic message about Yahuah's
character and plan. The name describes Yahuah, not the child. In
Isaiah's world, naming a child was never about who the child was; it was
about what Yahuah was doing in history. That is the key that unlocks
this verse.

***2.3 --- How the Verse Read Before the Doctrine***

To see how far apart the renderings are, set the familiar English
against the oldest translation we possess.

> **Isaiah 9:6 --- KJV**
>
> *"For unto us a child is born, unto us a son is given: and the
> government shall be upon his shoulder: and his name shall be called
> Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The
> Prince of Peace."*

The Septuagint is the Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures,
completed well over two centuries before the Messiah (Christ) was born
--- long before any creed about His nature existed. Watch what it does
with the very titles the doctrine leans on.

> **Isaiah 9:6 --- Septuagint (LXX)**
>
> *"Because a child was born to us; a son was given to us whose
> leadership came upon his shoulder; and his name is called Messenger of
> the Great Council, for I will bring peace upon the rulers and health
> to him."*

The English Bible hands the child four divine titles. The oldest Greek
translation removes "Mighty God" and "Everlasting Father" altogether and
gives the child a single title: Messenger of the Great Council. The
renderings differ because the Hebrew does not say what most English
Bibles claim. The translators made choices --- and those choices were
driven by doctrine, not grammar.

Part Three

**The Key Issues: Verb Tenses and the Name**

***3.1 --- Every Verb Has Been Changed***

This is the thing most readers will never hear from a pulpit. All the
verbs in Isaiah 9:6 are past tense in the Hebrew. Yet nearly every
English Bible changes them to the future. The word for "born" (yulad) is
the completed past form --- "was born." The word for "given" (nitan) is
also past --- "was given." And the word for "called" (vayikra) is a
narrative past form --- "and he called."

**וַיִּקְרָא**

***vayikra***

*and he called --- a narrative past form, used 208 times elsewhere and
rendered past every time but here*

Here is why that matters. The form vayikra ("and he called") appears in
this exact shape 208 other times in the Old Testament, and translators
render it as past tense every single time --- *Isaiah 9:6* is the only
place they switch it to the future. The verb for "born" (yulad) occurs
hundreds of times in the past, and this is the lone place it is given a
future sense. The same holds for "given." Out of hundreds upon hundreds
of uses, this is the one verse where the tense is changed. Why? Because
if the child "was born" and "was given," the verse is not a distant
prophecy about the Messiah --- it is about a child already born in
Isaiah's own day.

***3.2 --- The Greek Confirms the Past Tense***

The Septuagint, completed centuries before the Messiah and before any
debate about His nature, rendered every verb in Isaiah 9:6 as past: a
child was born to us; a son was given to us. Had the verbs been meant as
future, the Greek future forms were available and would have been used.
They were not. The oldest translation in existence describes a child who
had already been born.

***3.3 --- The "Prophetic Perfect" Defense***

Defenders of the doctrine know about the verb problem, so they answer
with the "prophetic perfect" --- a Hebrew idiom in which a prophet sees
a future event so vividly that he speaks of it as already done. The
idiom does exist, in rare cases. But it is something imposed on a text
when the natural past-tense reading does not fit a doctrine. Nothing in
Isaiah 9 signals the idiom. Everything in the context --- the Assyrian
war, the message to Ahaz, the sign-children --- points to events in
Isaiah's own lifetime.

Even a Christian scholar conceded the point. M. S. Terry, writing in
Moses and the Prophets (1901), said that the supposed instances of a
"prophetic perfect" in passages like *Isaiah 9:1-7* are no real
parallels, because their immediate context plainly prevents the
misreading. In plain words: the context of Isaiah 9 rules out a future
reading.

***3.4 --- Who Is Doing the Naming?***

Here is a detail the English hides. In the Hebrew, the verb "called"
comes before the noun, and Hebrew word order of this kind often marks
the one performing the action as the subject named first --- here,
Yahuah. Read straight from the Hebrew, the line runs: the Wonderful
Counselor, the Mighty God, the Everlasting Father called his name "The
Prince of Peace."

That changes everything. The string of divine titles is not a list of
names hung on the child. It is the description of the One doing the
naming --- Yahuah. The single name He gives the child is "The Prince of
Peace."

***3.5 --- The Word "Name" Is Singular***

One more detail seals it: the Hebrew word for "name" here (shemo) is
singular. Had Isaiah meant to hang four separate names on the child, the
plural form was available --- it appears scores of times elsewhere. But
here he uses the singular. The child has one name: Prince of Peace.

Part Four

**The Titles and the Child's Identity**

***4.1 --- What Is a Theophoric Name?***

A theophoric name is one that carries the divine name, or a description
of Yahuah, yet is given to a person. The name tells you something about
Yahuah, not about the one who bears it. If a hospital is named "God's
Healing Hand," no one thinks the building is God; the name describes
what God does through it.

Scripture is full of these. "Isaiah" (Yeshayahu) means "Yah has brought
deliverance" --- no one says Isaiah is Yahuah. "Elijah" (Eliyahu) means
"my God is Yah" --- no one says Elijah is God. "Immanuel" means "God is
with us" --- no one says the child of Isaiah 7 is God. Places, too:
Jerusalem is called Yahuah Tsidkenu, "Yahuah our Righteousness"
*Jeremiah 33:16*, yet the city is not Yahuah; Abraham's altar was called
Yahuah Yireh, "Yahuah will provide" *Genesis 22:14*, yet the altar is
not Yahuah. When you reach *Isaiah 9:6* the same pattern holds: the
compound name tells what Yahuah, the Mighty God and Everlasting Father,
does through this child, the Prince of Peace. It does not make the child
Yahuah.

***4.2 --- "El Gibbor" --- Does It Mean "Mighty God"?***

The whole weight of the argument rests on the phrase El Gibbor, rendered
"Mighty God" and offered as proof of deity. But the Hebrew El (Strong's
H410) does not always mean "God." Its range runs from mighty one, hero,
and man of rank, to ruler, to God. Context decides.

**אֵל גִּבּוֹר**

***El Gibbor***

*mighty one / mighty warrior --- 'El' ranges from ruler and hero to God,
decided by context*

And Scripture settles the range from within itself. In *Ezekiel 31:11*
the same word El is used of the king of Babylon and rendered "ruler." In
*Ezekiel 32:21* the form is used of heathen kings and rendered "mighty."
In *Psalm 82:1* human judges are called elohim --- and Yahushua himself
cited that line in *John 10:34-35*: "Is it not written in your law, I
said, ye are gods?" So the identical word is rendered "ruler" or
"mighty" for human leaders, then suddenly "God" when it lands in Isaiah
9:6. That is not translation; it is doctrine dressed up as translation.
Even mainstream Hebrew scholarship grants that a prophet would be
straining against all biblical usage to literally call a Davidic king
"God" --- "Mighty Hero" or "Divine Warrior" fits the language far
better.

***4.3 --- "Everlasting Father" --- The Self-Inflicted Wound***

This title turns the verse against the very doctrine it is summoned to
prove. The doctrine holds that the Son is not the Father --- two
separate persons. If the titles in Isaiah 9:6 describe the child, then
the child is being called "Father," and the child (Yahushua) is the
Father. That is a flat contradiction of the doctrine's own rule.

Only two exits remain. Either the child is the Father --- which is the
very position the creeds were written to condemn --- or the titles are
not descriptions of the child at all. The second is exactly what the
Hebrew says: Yahuah, the Everlasting Father, is the One doing the
naming.

It also helps to know that "father" was a common title for rulers and
leaders in the ancient world. *Isaiah 22:21* says of a government
official that he would be "a father to the inhabitants of Jerusalem."
*Job 29:16* says Job was "a father to the poor." That is leadership
language, not deity language.

***4.4 --- The Child Is Most Likely Hezekiah***

If the verbs are past tense and the passage concerns Isaiah's own day,
who is the child? The strongest candidate is Hezekiah, son of Ahaz. He
literally sat on the throne of David *Isaiah 9:7*. He brought peace and
justice to Judah, cleansed the temple, restored the appointed times, and
subdued the Philistines *2 Chronicles 29-32*. In his reign Yahuah
destroyed the Assyrian army that had threatened to wipe out Judah *2
Kings 18-19*. Scripture says of him that he trusted in Yahuah the God of
Israel, so that after him was none like him among all the kings of
Judah, nor among those before him *2 Kings 18:5*.

Even the name fits. "Hezekiah" (Chizkiyahu) joins chazak ("strong,
mighty") with Yah, the divine name --- "Mighty is Yahuah," or "Yahuah
gives strength." Set that beside El Gibbor, "Mighty God": the boy's own
name carries the same sense as the title in the verse. It makes no sense
for Isaiah to be pressing an urgent warning on Ahaz about Assyria, drop
in two verses about a distant Messiah, then return to the Assyrians. It
makes perfect sense if he is saying: your son has been born, Yahuah has
named him, and through him will come peace to Judah.

***4.5 --- The New Testament Never Uses This Verse to Prove Deity***

If *Isaiah 9:6* were the clear proof of the Messiah's deity it is
claimed to be, the apostles --- who quoted the Old Testament at every
turn --- would have leaned on it constantly. They never do. Not a single
New Testament writer ever quotes Isaiah 9:6 to prove Yahushua is Yahuah.
Yahushua is never called "Everlasting Father" anywhere in the New
Testament, and never called "Mighty God" (El Gibbor) anywhere in it. Had
these been his titles, the apostles would have used them. Their silence
is not an accident; it is evidence.

*Matthew 4:14-16* does quote *Isaiah 9:1-2* regarding Yahushua's
ministry beginning in Galilee --- but that is a geographical link, not a
claim of deity. Matthew never quotes verse 6, and never lays its titles
on Yahushua.

Part Five

**What the Text Actually Says**

***5 --- The Verse, Read Straight***

Read in the original Hebrew, Isaiah 9:6 says this: a child was born; a
son was given; the government was set on his shoulder; and the Wonderful
Counselor, the Mighty God, the Everlasting Father called his name "The
Prince of Peace."

The child is a Davidic king --- most likely Hezekiah --- born during the
Assyrian crisis. His name follows the same theophoric pattern as every
other sign-child in Isaiah 7--9. The divine titles describe Yahuah, who
names the child; the child's own title is "Prince of Peace." That
reading fits the Hebrew grammar, the immediate context, the oldest
translation, and the steady pattern of theophoric naming across
*Scripture*.

Conclusion

**The Verdict**

Isaiah 9:6 does not teach that the Messiah is Yahuah. It teaches that
Yahuah is mighty, that Yahuah is the everlasting Father, and that Yahuah
named a Davidic king "The Prince of Peace." The child is Yahuah's
anointed servant, not Yahuah Himself.

The Shema stands: "Hear, O Israel: Yahuah our God, Yahuah is one"
(Deuteronomy 6:4). He does not share His identity with another person.
He shares His power, His name, and His authority with those He sends ---
and that is exactly what this verse describes.

> **If Isaiah 9:6 proves the child is "Mighty God" and "Everlasting
> Father," then the doctrine must explain why the Son is the Father ---
> the very thing its own creed was written to deny.**

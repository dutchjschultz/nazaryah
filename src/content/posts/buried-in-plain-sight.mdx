## PART I: OPERATION PAPERCLIP — NAZIS AT THE HEART OF NASA

## The Program That Brought Nazis to America

In the spring of 1945, as Allied forces closed in on Nazi Germany, American military and intelligence officials were already planning for the postwar world. Rather than allow Germany’s most brilliant scientific minds to fall into Soviet hands, the United States launched a covert intelligence operation originally codenamed “Operation Overcast,” which was later renamed “Operation Paperclip.” The name reportedly came from the paperclips used to mark the files of scientists with particularly problematic Nazi backgrounds.

The program’s scope was staggering. Between 1945 and 1990, Operation Paperclip imported approximately 1,600 German scientists, engineers, and technicians to the United States. These were not random recruits—they were drawn from the Osenberg List, a catalogue of scientists and engineers who had been put to work for the Third Reich, recovered by Allied intelligence from a toilet at Bonn University.

President Harry Truman authorized the program with one key stipulation: no one who had been “a member of the Nazi Party, and more than a nominal participant in its activities, or an active supporter of Nazi militarism” was to be admitted. In practice, this restriction was systematically ignored. The Joint Intelligence Objectives Agency (JIOA), which managed the program, routinely altered, sanitized, or outright fabricated background files to make Nazi war criminals appear eligible for entry into the United States.

Born in 1912, von Braun was obsessed with rocketry from his teenage years. In late 1932, the German Army offered to fund his doctoral research in liquid-propellant rocketry—and shortly thereafter, Adolf Hitler became Chancellor of Germany.

Von Braun’s Nazi affiliations were deep and documented:

**Nazi Party Membership:** German documents show that von Braun himself applied for membership in the Nazi Party on November 12, 1937, despite his later claims that he was forced to join in 1939.

He later claimed to have left, but his SS number (185,068) was never reissued—indicating he never actually departed the organization. By 1943, he had risen to the rank of SS-Sturmbannführer (Major), a significant rank within Himmler’s feared Schutzstaffel. Photographs exist of von Braun proudly wearing his SS uniform alongside Heinrich Himmler himself.

Far more disturbing, as many as 20,000 concentration camp prisoners died assembling the weapons at the underground Mittelwerk factory. Yet this man—an SS officer, a Nazi Party member, and a direct beneficiary of concentration camp slave labor—was brought to America, given a new identity, and placed at the very heart of the U.S. space program.

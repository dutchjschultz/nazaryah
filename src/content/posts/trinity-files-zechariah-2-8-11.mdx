**An Angel Sent by Yahuah, Not a Second Yahuah**

*Reading the Sender and the Sent in Zechariah 2:8--11*

An examination of text, context, and the principle of divine agency

--- The Standing Stone ---

> **Behind "LORD" in your Bible lies a hidden name --- in the Hebrew it is Yahuah** *Psalm 83:18***; Yahuah is the Father** *Isaiah 63:16***; Yahuah is the only God, beside Him there is no other** *Isaiah 45:5***; therefore Yahuah the Father is the only true God, leaving no room for a second or third person** *1 Corinthians 8:6***.**

Part One

**The Claim on the Table**

***1.1 --- What Is Claimed About This Passage***

Zechariah 2:10--11 reads:

> **Zechariah 2:10--11**
>
> *Sing and rejoice, O daughter of Zion: for, lo, I come, and I will dwell in the midst of thee, saith the LORD \[Yahuah\]. And many nations shall be joined to the LORD \[Yahuah\] in that day, and shall be my people: and I will dwell in the midst of thee, and thou shalt know that the LORD \[Yahuah\] of hosts hath sent me unto thee.*

Defenders of a plural Godhead fasten on one phrase: "the LORD of hosts hath sent me." They argue the speaker calls himself Yahuah and then says Yahuah sent him --- one divine person named Yahuah, sent by another divine person also named Yahuah. Two persons, both called Yahuah, supposedly proving a plurality within God.

They also point to what they call the "He--Me pattern." The speaker moves back and forth between the first person ("I will dwell") and the third person ("the LORD"). To them, that shifting proves more than one person stands behind the words.

***1.2 --- The Problem Before We Even Look at the Verses***

Before we open the Hebrew, notice one thing. *Hebrews 1:1-2* says Yahuah spake "in time past unto the fathers by the prophets," but "hath in these last days spoken unto us by his Son." The pattern is plain. In the Old Testament Yahuah communicated through prophets and angels; He did not speak through the Son until the last days. So when Zechariah records someone being "sent" by Yahuah, the natural candidate is a prophet or an angel --- not the Son.

And that is exactly what the text tells us. The speaker in Zechariah 2 is identified as an angel. The plural-God reading has to ignore that identification before its argument can even leave the ground.

Part Two

**Verse-by-Verse Examination**

***2.1 --- The Context Names the Speaker as an Angel***

The most basic problem with the plural reading is that the text itself tells us who is speaking. Zechariah 2:3 says:

> **Zechariah 2:3**
>
> *And, behold, the angel that talked with me went forth, and another angel went out to meet him.*

Two angels. That is the setting. The whole book of Zechariah is built around angelic messengers carrying Yahuah's words to the prophet. In *Zechariah 1:9* the prophet asks the angel, "O my lord, what are these?" and the angel explains the vision. In *Zechariah 1:14* Yahuah gives a message to the angel, and the angel turns to Zechariah and says, "Thus saith the LORD \[Yahuah\] of hosts; I am jealous for Jerusalem and for Zion with a great jealousy."

Notice what just happened. The angel received a message from Yahuah. Then he delivered it in the first person --- "I am jealous." The angel said "I" even though the words belong to Yahuah. No one claims this angel is Yahuah. He is speaking for Yahuah --- a messenger carrying another's message. That is precisely what happens in Zechariah 2.

***2.2 --- The "Sent Me" Statement Is the Angel's Validation***

When the speaker in *Zechariah 2:9* and *Zechariah 2:11* says, "ye shall know that the LORD \[Yahuah\] of hosts hath sent me," this is the angel saying: when these things come true, you will know God sent me to tell you of them. The "me" is the angel. The sender is Yahuah. It is a validation statement, not a claim of deity. There is no second Yahuah in the passage.

Part Three

**The Prophetic Messenger Formula**

***3.1 --- Prophets Speak as Yahuah Without Being Yahuah***

The strongest evidence against the plural reading is a pattern that runs all through the Hebrew Bible --- the prophetic messenger formula. Here is how it works. Yahuah gives a message to a prophet or angel. The prophet or angel then delivers it in the first person, saying "I" as though he were Yahuah himself. This does not mean the prophet is Yahuah. It means he speaks for Yahuah --- the same way an ancient messenger delivered a king's words, standing before the court and saying "I decree this," though the king is the one who decreed it.

This happens hundreds of times in the Old Testament. Look at *Haggai 1:13*. The prophet Haggai is called Yahuah's messenger --- in Hebrew, His malak, the very word used of angels:

**מַלְאָךְ**

***malak***

*messenger; one sent --- used of both angels and prophets (Strong's H4397)*

Then Haggai speaks Yahuah's words in the first person: "I am with you, saith the LORD \[Yahuah\]." Haggai says "I am with you" on Yahuah's behalf. No one claims Haggai is a second Yahuah. In *Jeremiah 1:9* Yahuah says, "I have put my words in thy mouth." In *Ezekiel 2:7* He tells the prophet, "Thou shalt speak my words unto them." That is how prophetic speech works: Yahuah's words come through human or angelic agents who speak them in the first person. The agent is not Yahuah; the agent carries Yahuah's authority and speaks Yahuah's words.

***3.2 --- A Known Principle of Agency in the Ancient World***

The "sent me" pattern fits a principle well known across the ancient Near East: an authorized messenger represents the one who sent him. The messenger may even speak in the sender's name and use the sender's words --- yet this never makes the messenger the same person as the sender. It only means he has been given authority to speak on the sender's behalf.

That is why Zechariah's angel keeps saying, "ye shall know that the LORD \[Yahuah\] of hosts hath sent me." The phrase is a boundary marker. It draws a clean line between the sender (Yahuah) and the sent one (the angel). The very sentence used to prove two Yahuahs proves the opposite: one Yahuah who sends, and one messenger who is sent.

Part Four

**Angels Who Speak as Yahuah**

***4.1 --- Genesis 22: The Angel on Mount Moriah***

The messenger formula is not limited to human prophets; angels do the same thing, and that bears directly on Zechariah 2. In *Genesis 22:11-12* the angel of Yahuah calls to Abraham: "Lay not thine hand upon the lad... for now I know that thou fearest God, seeing thou hast not withheld thy son, thine only son from me." Then in verses 16--17 the same angel says, "By myself have I sworn, saith the LORD \[Yahuah\]... That in blessing I will bless thee."

The angel says "from me," "by myself have I sworn," and "I will bless thee." He speaks entirely in the first person, as if he were Yahuah. Yet Genesis 22:11 says plainly that the speaker is "the angel of the LORD \[Yahuah\]" --- a messenger. He carries Yahuah's authority and speaks Yahuah's words, but he is not Yahuah himself.

***4.2 --- Exodus 3: The Burning Bush***

*Exodus 3:2* says "the angel of the LORD \[Yahuah\] appeared unto him in a flame of fire out of the midst of a bush." Then in verse 6 the speaker says, "I am the God of thy father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob." The angel speaks Yahuah's words as Yahuah.

Here is the key: the New Testament settles it for us. In *Acts 7:30* Stephen says "there appeared to him in the wilderness of mount Sina an angel of the Lord in a flame of fire in a bush." And in *Acts 7:35* he says Yahuah sent Moses "by the hand of the angel which appeared to him in the bush." Stephen, full of the Holy Spirit, identifies the burning-bush figure as an angel --- not as a pre-incarnate second person of a Godhead.

***4.3 --- Judges 2: The Closest Parallel***

Perhaps the closest parallel to Zechariah 2 is *Judges 2:1*. The angel of Yahuah says:

> **Judges 2:1**
>
> *I made you to go up out of Egypt, and have brought you unto the land which I sware unto your fathers; and I said, I will never break my covenant with you.*

Read it again. The angel says he brought Israel out of Egypt. The angel says he swore the oath to the fathers. The angel says he made the covenant. These are all Yahuah's acts, spoken in Yahuah's voice, through Yahuah's messenger. The angel is not Yahuah; he is Yahuah's authorized representative, delivering the message in the first person --- exactly the way ancient messengers worked, and exactly what happens in Zechariah 2.

***4.4 --- The Same "Sent Me" Formula Elsewhere in Zechariah***

Here is evidence the plural reading almost never addresses. The same phrase --- "ye shall know that the LORD of hosts hath sent me unto you" --- appears twice more in Zechariah. *Zechariah 4:9* says:

> **Zechariah 4:9**
>
> *The hands of Zerubbabel have laid the foundation of this house; his hands shall also finish it; and thou shalt know that the LORD \[Yahuah\] of hosts hath sent me unto you.*

*Zechariah 6:15* says:

> **Zechariah 6:15**
>
> *And they that are far off shall come and build in the temple of the LORD \[Yahuah\], and ye shall know that the LORD \[Yahuah\] of hosts hath sent me unto you.*

In both places the speaker is the angelic messenger. The formula works the same way: when these prophecies come true, you will know that Yahuah sent me. No one --- not even the most committed defender of a plural Godhead --- claims that Zechariah 4:9 or 6:15 describes a second Yahuah. Same formula, same speaker, same function. There is no reason to read Zechariah 2:9 and 2:11 any differently.

Part Five

**Quotation Marks, the Greek, and Illeism**

***5.1 --- Where the Quotation Marks Fall***

Much of the confusion comes from where English Bibles place their quotation marks. The NET Bible is helpful here, because it shows exactly where Yahuah's direct speech ends and where the angel begins speaking in his own voice:

> **Zechariah 2:10--11 (NET)**
>
> *'Sing out and be happy, Zion my daughter! For look, I have come; I will settle in your midst,' says the LORD. 'Many nations will join themselves to the Lord on the day of salvation, and they will also be my people. Indeed, I will settle in the midst of you all.' Then you will know that the Lord who rules over all has sent me to you.*

See the last sentence? "Then you will know that the Lord who rules over all has sent me to you" stands outside Yahuah's quoted speech. It is the angel's own statement --- his personal note that Yahuah is the one who sent him with this message. Once you see where the quotation marks fall, the whole "two Yahuahs" idea dissolves.

***5.2 --- The Greek Keeps the Same Distinction***

The Septuagint is the ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures, finished centuries before Yahushua (Jesus) was born. In its rendering of this passage it keeps the clear distinction between Yahuah of hosts (the sender) and the one who is sent. The verse still reads as a messenger speaking with divine authority while plainly naming Yahuah of hosts as the sender. So the "two speakers" feature is not an invention of English translators --- it is built into the prophetic style itself, and the oldest translation we possess understood it that way.

***5.3 --- Yahuah Speaking of Himself in the Third Person***

Some are thrown by the way the passage shifts between "I" and "the LORD." But this is a known feature of Hebrew writing called illeism --- a speaker referring to himself in the third person. Yahuah does it throughout the Old Testament. Look at *Zechariah 3:2*, the very next chapter: "And the LORD \[Yahuah\] said unto Satan, The LORD \[Yahuah\] rebuke thee, O Satan; even the LORD \[Yahuah\] that hath chosen Jerusalem rebuke thee." Yahuah is speaking --- and He names Himself "the LORD" in the third person. No one argues this proves two Yahuahs in Zechariah 3.

*Hosea 1:7* is sharper still: "But I will have mercy upon the house of Judah, and will save them by the LORD \[Yahuah\] their God." Yahuah is speaking, and in one sentence He uses both the first person ("I will... save them") and the third person ("by the LORD their God") --- both referring to the same God. *Genesis 18:19* does the same: "For I know him, that he will command his children... to keep the way of the LORD \[Yahuah\]." Yahuah says "I know him" and then names Himself "the LORD." It is a style of speech, not a head-count of persons.

***5.4 --- The New Testament Confirms the Agency Model***

The New Testament backs the agency reading, not the plural one. As we saw, Stephen identifies the burning-bush speaker as an angel (*Acts 7:30*, *Acts 7:35*). Paul says in *Galatians 3:19* that the law "was ordained by angels in the hand of a mediator." *Acts 7:38* describes Moses "in the church in the wilderness with the angel which spake to him in the mount Sina." The pattern is consistent: Old Testament angelic speakers are treated as agents of Yahuah who carry His authority and speak His words --- never as Yahuah Himself appearing as a second divine person.

Conclusion

**The Verdict**

Zechariah 2:8--11 shows a scene we meet all through the Hebrew Bible. Yahuah gives a message. An angel carries it to the prophet and speaks Yahuah's words in the first person, as every messenger does. Then he adds his own note: when these things come true, you will know that Yahuah sent me. One God. One angel. One message. The promise on its face is Yahuah's restored presence --- "I will dwell in the midst of thee" --- not the arrival of a second Yahuah.

The agency model is no modern invention. It is one of the oldest and most settled patterns in Scripture. Prophets speak for Yahuah; angels speak for Yahuah. They say "I" because they carry His words, and they say "He sent me" because they know the difference between themselves and the God they serve. So the Shema still stands undisturbed: "Hear, O Israel: the LORD \[Yahuah\] our God is one LORD \[Yahuah\]" (Deuteronomy 6:4).

> **If an angel saying "the LORD of hosts hath sent me" in Zechariah 2:11 proves a second Yahuah, then the identical words in Zechariah 4:9 and 6:15 prove a third and a fourth --- and no Trinitarian is willing to go there.**

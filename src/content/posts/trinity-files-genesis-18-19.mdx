**Genesis 18--19**

**Three Came to the Tent**

*The visitor who bore the Name was sent by the One who owns it*

A messenger can carry the Name of Yahuah without becoming a second
Yahuah.

--- The Standing Stone ---

> **Behind "LORD" in your Bible lies a hidden name --- in the Hebrew it
> is Yahuah** *Psalm 83:18***; Yahuah is the Father** *Isaiah 63:16***;
> Yahuah is the only God, beside Him there is no other** *Isaiah
> 45:5***; therefore Yahuah the Father is the only true God, leaving no
> room for a second or third person** *1 Corinthians 8:6***.**

Part One

**The Claim and Its Problems**

***1 --- The Claim Stated***

Defenders of a plural Godhead have argued that one of the three visitors
who appeared to Abraham in Genesis 18 was actually a pre-incarnate
appearance of Yahushua (Jesus) --- that is, God the Son showing up in
visible form before Bethlehem. Their reasoning goes like this: since the
text calls the visitor who stayed with Abraham "Yahuah" (Genesis 18:22),
and since "no one has ever seen God" (John 1:18), which they take to
mean no one has seen God the Father, then the visible Yahuah must be the
second person of the Trinity --- God the Son.

They also point to Genesis 19:24, where the divine name appears twice in
the same verse, as proof that two distinct divine persons are working
together.

This study will show that this reading fails on every level. It
contradicts Trinitarian theology itself. It ignores the well-known
biblical principle of divine agency. It cannot survive a plain reading
of Genesis 18--19. And it is ruled out by the New Testament's own words.

***2 --- The Pre-Incarnation Problem***

The first problem is one that Trinitarians themselves should catch,
because it comes from inside their own doctrine. According to standard
Trinitarian teaching, the Son did not take on human form until the
incarnation --- an event roughly two thousand years after Abraham.
Before that, the Son was held to be fully God, sharing every divine
quality equally with the Father and the Holy Spirit.

Now think about that. If the Son had not yet become human, then in that
framework he was just as invisible and unapproachable as the Father.
Exodus 33:20 says no one can see God and live. If the Father, Son, and
Holy Spirit are truly co-equal in every divine quality, then this rule
should apply to all three of them equally. Why would you be able to see
"God the Son" and survive but not "God the Father"? Either all three are
equally unseeable, or none of them are. You cannot have it both ways.

Here is another way to see the problem. If the pre-incarnate Son could
just show up in visible form whenever he wanted, then what makes the
incarnation special? Why would the writer of Hebrews present it as
something remarkable and unprecedented if the Son had already been
appearing to people for centuries?

***3 --- The Testimony of Hebrews 1:1--2***

The writer of Hebrews opens with a statement that speaks directly to
this question:

> **Hebrews 1:1--2**
>
> *God, who at sundry times and in divers manners spake in time past
> unto the fathers by the prophets, hath in these last days spoken unto
> us by his Son...*

Pay close attention to the structure of that sentence. There are two
time periods. In the past, God spoke through the prophets. Now, in these
last days, God speaks through the Son. The shift is deliberate.
Something has changed.

Think about what this means. If the Son had been the one speaking to
Abraham, to Moses on Sinai, to Isaiah, and to all the prophets
throughout the Old Testament, then this contrast makes no sense. The
writer would never set "in time past... by the prophets" against "in
these last days... by his Son" if the Son had been the primary speaker
all along. That statement only works if speaking through the Son is
something new.

The claim that the Son was active and visible throughout the Old
Testament --- visiting Abraham, giving the law at Sinai, appearing as
the angel of Yahuah --- makes Hebrews 1:1--2 meaningless. Every honest
reader can see that.

Part Two

**The Three Messengers in Genesis 18--19**

***4 --- The Arrival (Genesis 18:1--2)***

> **Genesis 18:1--2**
>
> *And the LORD \[Yahuah\] appeared unto him in the plains of Mamre: and
> he sat in the tent door in the heat of the day; and he lift up his
> eyes and looked, and, lo, three men stood by him...*

The passage says Yahuah appeared to Abraham. Then it tells us what
Abraham actually sees: three men. Not "Yahuah and two angels." Not
"Yahuah with two companions." Just three men. The appearance of Yahuah
is carried out through the presence of these three figures. This is the
first clue that we are looking at representative agency --- not Yahuah
showing up in person.

This is exactly how the Hebrew Scriptures regularly describe divine
encounters. Yahuah "appears" to people through his messengers. In Exodus
3:2, the angel of Yahuah shows up in the burning bush. But by verse 4,
God called to Moses out of the bush. The messenger carries the presence
and voice of the One who sent him. The same thing is happening here.

***5 --- The Witness of the Greek***

The oldest Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures preserves the same
basic story: heavenly messengers appear, speak with divine authority,
and carry out judgment. It does not force the reader to conclude that
one of the visitors is a second divine person who is Yahuah in essence.
The account remains what it has always been --- a messenger encounter,
not a split within God.

***6 --- The Meal and Conversation (Genesis 18:3--15)***

Abraham prepares a meal for all three visitors. He serves them together.
They eat together (verse 8), they ask about Sarah (verse 9), and they
deliver the promise of a son. Throughout this section the three men act
as a single group. There is nothing in the text that sets one of them
apart as different from the other two.

At verse 10 one of them says he will surely return about that time the
next year, and Sarah will have a son. This is a promise that belongs to
Yahuah. But that does not mean the speaker is Yahuah in person. Think of
it this way: when a prophet says "Thus saith the LORD, I will bless
you," nobody thinks the prophet is Yahuah. The messenger speaks with the
authority of the One who sent him. That is all that is happening here.

***7 --- The Departure Toward Sodom (Genesis 18:16--22)***

This is where careful reading becomes critical. Follow the action step
by step.

> **Genesis 18:16**
>
> *And the men rose up from thence, and looked toward Sodom: and Abraham
> went with them to bring them on the way.*

All three men get up. All three look toward Sodom. All three are heading
out.

> **Genesis 18:20--21**
>
> *And the LORD \[Yahuah\] said, Because the cry of Sodom and Gomorrah
> is great, and because their sin is very grievous; I will go down now,
> and see whether they have done altogether according to the cry of
> it...*

Yahuah announces: "I will go down." Hold that thought.

> **Genesis 18:22**
>
> *And the men turned their faces from thence, and went toward Sodom:
> but Abraham stood yet before the LORD \[Yahuah\].*

Now connect the dots. Yahuah says "I will go down" --- and then the text
says the men turned and went toward Sodom. That is how Yahuah "goes
down": through his messengers. At the same time, one of the three stays
behind with Abraham, and this is described as Abraham standing before
Yahuah.

So Yahuah is represented in two places at once. Two men go to Sodom ---
that is Yahuah going down. One man stays with Abraham --- that is Yahuah
remaining present with Abraham. The agents carry his name, his
authority, and his presence wherever they go.

Trinitarians read this and say the one who stayed must be Yahuah himself
--- the pre-incarnate Christ --- while the other two are just angels.
But that reading ignores something obvious. Yahuah said "I will go
down," and the ones who go down are the two angels. If Yahuah's trip to
Sodom is accomplished by two angels, then Yahuah's presence with Abraham
is equally accomplished by the one angel who stays behind.

***8 --- The Two Angels Arrive (Genesis 19:1)***

> **Genesis 19:1**
>
> *And there came two angels to Sodom at even; and Lot sat in the gate
> of Sodom...*

The "men" who left for Sodom in 18:22 are now called what they are: two
angels. So out of the original three men, two are confirmed angels. The
math is simple. Three men arrived (18:2). Two are angels (19:1). If two
out of three are angels, the most natural conclusion is that the third
one is also an angel serving in the same role.

***9 --- Lot's Reverence (Genesis 19:1--2)***

> **Genesis 19:1**
>
> *...and Lot seeing them rose up to meet them; and he bowed himself
> with his face toward the ground.*

Lot bows face-down before two angels. The Greek translation uses the
word proskuneo here --- the same word used for worship throughout the
New Testament, including in Hebrews 1:6 where angels worship the Son.

This matters because some Trinitarians argue from Revelation 19:10 and
22:8--9 that bowing before angels is wrong, and so anyone who is bowed
to must be God. But here Lot bows before angels and nobody corrects him.
Many passages in the Hebrew Scriptures show men bowing before other men,
kings, and angelic figures. The word proskuneo does not automatically
mean worship of God. It can simply mean deep respect for someone in
authority.

***10 --- The Angels Speak as Yahuah (Genesis 19:13--14)***

> **Genesis 19:13**
>
> *For we will destroy this place, because the cry of them is waxen
> great before the face of the LORD \[Yahuah\]; and the LORD \[Yahuah\]
> hath sent us to destroy it.*

Notice the two statements the angels make. First: "We will destroy this
place." They claim the action for themselves. Second: "Yahuah hath sent
us to destroy it." They explain where their authority comes from. They
are agents acting under divine orders.

> **Genesis 19:14**
>
> *And Lot went out... Up, get you out of this place; for the LORD
> \[Yahuah\] will destroy this city...*

Now watch how Lot processes what he just heard. The angels said "we will
destroy." Lot tells his family "Yahuah will destroy." To Lot, the
angels' action is Yahuah's action. He sees no difference. The angels
destroy equals Yahuah destroys. This is the agency principle
demonstrated right inside the text, not imported from anywhere else.

***11 --- Yahuah's Compassion Through Angels (Genesis 19:16)***

> **Genesis 19:16**
>
> *And while he lingered, the men laid hold upon his hand... the LORD
> \[Yahuah\] being merciful unto him: and they brought him forth, and
> set him without the city.*

The angels are the ones who seize Lot's hand and physically bring him
out. But the writer says this happened because of Yahuah's mercy. What
the angels do, the text credits to Yahuah --- not because the angels are
Yahuah, but because they are carrying out his will.

Notice also that the destruction has not happened yet. The angels are
still in rescue mode. The authority stays with Yahuah in heaven. The
execution is carried out by his agents on the ground.

***12 --- Lot's Address (Genesis 19:18--21)***

> **Genesis 19:18**
>
> *And Lot said unto them, Oh, not so, my Lord...*

Lot calls the angel adon --- a Hebrew word that simply means "my lord"
or "sir." This is not the divine name. It is the kind of respectful
address a servant gives a master or a subject gives a king. Do not
confuse this with Lot addressing Yahuah himself.

> **Genesis 19:21**
>
> *And he said unto him, See, I have accepted thee concerning this thing
> also, that I will not overthrow this city, for the which thou hast
> spoken.*

Here the angel speaks in the first person as Yahuah's agent: "I have
accepted thee... I will not overthrow this city." This is standard
messenger speech in the Hebrew Scriptures. The messenger speaks as if he
were the sender himself. Think of a royal ambassador who says "I decree"
when delivering the king's decree. The ambassador is not the king. He is
speaking the king's words.

The Trinitarian reading would have us believe that Lot --- a man who had
to be physically dragged out of the city, who bargains about which small
town to run to --- is having a casual back-and-forth conversation with
the Almighty Creator of heaven and earth. That is a stretch. The far
simpler reading is that Lot is talking to an angelic agent who has the
authority to relay Yahuah's mercy.

Part Three

**Genesis 19:24 --- "Yahuah from Yahuah"**

***13 --- The Verse***

> **Genesis 19:24**
>
> *Then the LORD \[Yahuah\] rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah
> brimstone and fire from the LORD \[Yahuah\] out of heaven.*

This is the big verse. Trinitarians hold it up as proof of two divine
persons: a "Yahuah" on earth raining fire, and a "Yahuah out of heaven"
from whom the fire comes. They say the Yahuah on earth is the
pre-incarnate Son, and the Yahuah in heaven is the Father.

But does this verse actually require that reading? No. And a simple
everyday example shows why.

***14 --- How the Writer Uses the Name Twice***

Picture this sentence: "And then Bob spoke to the store owner fiery
words from the mouth of Bob."

Nobody reading that would think there are two Bobs. The first "Bob"
tells you who is performing the action. The second "Bob" tells you where
the action comes from --- it is Bob's own mouth, Bob's own authority.
The sentence has a little drama and flair, but it is obviously talking
about one person described from two angles: the actor and the source.

This is exactly what the writer of Genesis is doing. He uses Yahuah's
name twice, not because there are two Yahuahs, but because he is
describing the same Yahuah in two ways. The first mention ("Yahuah
rained upon Sodom") describes Yahuah as the one carrying out the
judgment --- through his angelic agents on the ground. The second
mention ("from Yahuah out of heaven") describes Yahuah as the ultimate
source behind the judgment --- the fire and brimstone come from him.

This is not Yahuah talking about himself in the third person. It is the
narrator describing Yahuah's action from two vantage points: the
execution on earth, carried out by agents, and the origin in heaven,
from Yahuah's own authority. The double reference drives home the
completeness of the act. Yahuah is both the commander and the source.

***15 --- The Context Already Told Us How to Read It***

We do not even need to guess about the meaning of Genesis 19:24. The
verses right before it already showed us the pattern. In Genesis 19:13
the angels say, "We will destroy this place... Yahuah hath sent us to
destroy it." In Genesis 19:14 Lot says, "Yahuah will destroy the city."
In Genesis 19:24 the narrator says, "Yahuah rained upon Sodom... from
Yahuah out of heaven."

The pattern is seamless. The angels' action is Yahuah's action. When the
angels say "we will destroy," Lot understands it as "Yahuah will
destroy," and the narrator writes it as "Yahuah rained... from Yahuah."
There is no second divine person in this story. There is one Yahuah
acting through his authorized agents, and the narrator credits the act
to Yahuah both as the one carrying it out, through agents, and the one
it comes from, out of heaven.

***16 --- The Same Pattern Elsewhere in Scripture***

This way of writing is not unique to Genesis 19:24. Other passages do
the same thing.

In Hosea 1:7, Yahuah is the speaker, and he says he will save the house
of Judah "by the LORD their God." Yahuah says he will save them by
Yahuah. The structure is the same as Genesis 19:24: the speaker performs
an action and attributes the means to Yahuah. No Trinitarian argues
there are two Yahuahs in Hosea 1:7. They accept it as God referring to
himself. The same standard should apply to Genesis 19:24.

In 1 Kings 8:1, "Solomon assembled the elders of Israel... unto king
Solomon." Solomon... unto Solomon. The narrator repeats the proper name
instead of using "him." This is ordinary Hebrew style --- repeating the
name for clarity and emphasis. Nobody concludes there are two Solomons.

Trinitarians apply a standard to Genesis 19:24 that they refuse to apply
to any other verse in Scripture. That inconsistency alone should raise a
red flag.

***17 --- Abraham's Return (Genesis 19:27--28)***

> **Genesis 19:27**
>
> *And Abraham gat up early in the morning to the place where he stood
> before the LORD \[Yahuah\]...*

When Abraham goes back, the text says he had stood before Yahuah ---
singular. Not "before Yahuah the Son while Yahuah the Father was in
heaven." Not "before one of the two Yahuahs." Just one Yahuah. If the
Trinitarian reading were correct, you would expect the narrative to keep
that distinction. It does not. Because there is only one Yahuah.

Part Four

**The Principle of Divine Agency**

***18 --- What Angels Are***

The Hebrew word for angel, mal'akh, simply means "messenger." That is
all it means. Angels are Yahuah's messengers, sent to deliver his word
and carry out his will. As his authorized representatives, they carry
his name, speak in his voice, and act with his authority. This is not a
small detail. It is central to how the Hebrew Scriptures portray
encounters between Yahuah and people.

The principle is straightforward. In the ancient world the agent of a
person was treated as the person himself. A king's messenger spoke the
king's words and carried the king's authority. To receive the messenger
was to receive the king. To reject the messenger was to reject the king.
Yet even when the messenger spoke with full authority, nobody confused
the messenger with the king himself.

Yahushua himself confirms this exact principle: "He that receiveth you
receiveth me, and he that receiveth me receiveth him that sent me"
(Matthew 10:40). The sent one represents the sender. That does not make
them the same person.

***19 --- Angels Speaking as Yahuah: Biblical Examples***

Angels in the Hebrew Scriptures regularly speak in the first person as
Yahuah. The examples are clear. In Genesis 22:11--12 the angel of Yahuah
calls from heaven and says, "Now I know that thou fearest God" --- the
angel speaks as if he were Yahuah. In Exodus 3:2--4 the angel of Yahuah
appears in the burning bush, and then God calls to Moses out of the bush
--- the messenger carries the voice of the sender. In Judges 2:1 the
angel of Yahuah says, "I made you to go up out of Egypt... I said, I
will never break my covenant with you" --- the angel speaks Yahuah's
words in the first person. In Judges 6:12--14 the angel of Yahuah
appears to Gideon, and then Yahuah turns to him and speaks; the text
switches between "angel" and "Yahuah" because the agent speaks for the
sender.

These are not cases of Yahuah literally standing in a bush or at a
threshing floor. They are cases of his messengers carrying his presence
and speaking his words. Genesis 18--19 follows the same pattern.

***20 --- The Ancient World Understood This***

This was not unusual in the ancient world. Royal messengers routinely
delivered their king's words in the first person. The envoy would say
"I" because the king's authority was being delivered, even though the
envoy and the king were obviously not the same person. It is the same
pattern we see with the prophets who say "Thus saith the LORD, I
will..." The prophet speaks God's "I" without being God. Genesis 18--19
fits this broader pattern of speech perfectly.

***21 --- Yahushua and the Agency Model***

What is remarkable is that Yahushua describes his own relationship to
the Father in terms that mirror this exact agency pattern. In the Gospel
of John he says it over and over:

> **John 14:10**
>
> *...the words that I speak unto you I speak not of myself: but the
> Father that dwelleth in me, he doeth the works.*
>
> **John 7:16**
>
> *...My doctrine is not mine, but his that sent me.*
>
> **John 12:49**
>
> *For I have not spoken of myself; but the Father which sent me, he
> gave me a commandment, what I should say, and what I should speak.*

If this is how Yahushua describes his own work --- as one who speaks the
Father's words and does the Father's works --- then how much more does
this same model apply to angels? The messengers of Yahuah speak his
words, carry his authority, and act in his name. When the text calls
them "Yahuah," it is because they are faithfully representing him, not
because they actually are him.

***22 --- The New Testament Confirms the Pattern***

The New Testament keeps these same categories. Angels are real personal
beings, sent by Yahuah, who can speak for him without being him. Hebrews
13:2 reminds believers that some have entertained angels unawares ---
which fits Abraham's encounter perfectly. And when Stephen retells the
history of Israel in Acts 7, he repeatedly identifies the one who spoke
to Moses as "the angel" --- not Yahuah in person, but an angel who spoke
on Yahuah's behalf.

***23 --- Children as Representations***

There is one more layer to this. Angels are also called "sons of God" in
the Hebrew Scriptures (Job 1:6, 2:1, 38:7). As children of Yahuah, they
represent their Father. They carry his character, reflect his will, and
make his purposes known. This is the deepest layer of the agency
principle: the messengers do not just carry a message, they make Yahuah
known. They are not Yahuah himself, but the text can identify their
actions with his because they faithfully represent him.

Part Five

**The Case Summarized**

***24 --- What the Text Actually Says***

When we read Genesis 18--19 carefully, here is what the text actually
tells us. Three men appear to Abraham (18:2). All three eat with him.
All three rise and look toward Sodom (18:16). Yahuah says "I will go
down" to Sodom (18:21). Two of the three depart toward Sodom; one stays
with Abraham (18:22). The two who arrive in Sodom are called two angels
(19:1). These two angels say both "we will destroy" and "Yahuah hath
sent us" (19:13). Lot understands the angels' mission as Yahuah's own
action: "Yahuah will destroy the city" (19:14). Yahuah's compassion is
expressed through the angels physically rescuing Lot (19:16). The
narrator describes the destruction as "Yahuah rained... from Yahuah out
of heaven" (19:24), crediting the act to Yahuah as both the executor,
through agents, and the source, out of heaven. Abraham returns to the
spot where he stood before Yahuah --- singular (19:27).

Three men. All angels. All representing Yahuah. Two go to Sodom to carry
out his judgment. One stays with Abraham as his presence. Two plus one
is three. All three are angels. All three represent Yahuah. None of them
is Yahuah.

Conclusion

**The Verdict**

Genesis 18--19 does not teach a plurality of divine persons. It teaches
what the whole of the Hebrew Scriptures teaches: that Yahuah is one,
that he acts in the world through messengers who carry his name and
authority, and that his agents' actions are rightly credited to him
because he is the source of their mission, their words, and their power.

The reading level required is not advanced. Three men came to Abraham.
They were three angels. Two went to Sodom. One stayed. All three
represented Yahuah. And Yahuah --- the one God of Israel --- rained
judgment from heaven on Sodom and Gomorrah through the agents he had
sent. "Hear, O Israel: the LORD \[Yahuah\] our God is one LORD
\[Yahuah\]" (Deuteronomy 6:4).

> **If Yahuah's "going down" to Sodom is accomplished by two angels,
> then his "remaining" with Abraham is accomplished by the third --- and
> the case for a second divine person is left with three messengers and
> not one pre-incarnate Christ.**

CHAPTER 1

**"Yahuah Said to My Lord"**

*A Rebuttal of the Trinitarian Reading of Psalm 110:1*

Hebrew Wording, Royal Context, and the Apostolic Verdict on the Messiah’s Throne

**Trinitarian Argument Strength:** ★★☆☆☆  2 out of 5

*The verse is well-known and sounds impressive at first, but the Hebrew wording, the royal psalm genre, and Peter’s plain interpretation in Acts 2:36 all work against the Trinitarian reading. It collapses quickly under close examination.*

PART ONE

**Framing the Trinitarian Claim**

## 1.1 — The Verse Under Examination

*"The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool." — Psalm 110:1 (KJV)*

This single verse is one of the most quoted Old Testament passages in the New Testament. It shows up in the Gospels, in Acts, in Paul’s letters, and in Hebrews. Because of that, Trinitarians lean on it heavily. Their argument goes like this: two separate “Lords” appear in the verse, and since one of them is Yahuah (**LORD** in the KJV), the second “Lord” must also be a divine person. They then connect this to Yahushua (Jesus) being called “Lord” in the New Testament and conclude that the Messiah must be a second person within one God.

## 1.2 — The Problems Before We Even Open the Hebrew

Before we even look at the original language, the Trinitarian reading has a built-in problem. If the second “Lord” in this verse is Yahuah Himself, then the text would be saying Yahuah spoke to Yahuah. But that is not what the text says. The whole point of the verse is that there are **two different figures** — the one who speaks (Yahuah) and the one who sits (the appointed ruler). Two different persons, yes. Two different Gods, or two persons in one God? The text never says that. It says one is giving orders and the other is receiving a seat. That is the language of a king being installed by Yahuah — not the language of two co-equal divine persons having a conversation.

Think of it this way. If your boss says, “Sit at the head of the table until I finish dealing with the competition,” nobody would assume you and your boss are the same person. One is giving the order. The other is receiving it. That is exactly what Psalm 110:1 describes.

PART TWO

**Verse-by-Verse Examination**

## 2.1 — The Hebrew Behind “My Lord” — Adoni vs. Adonai

This is the single most important detail in the entire debate, and it is hiding in plain sight. In Hebrew, the word translated “my Lord” in Psalm 110:1 is *adoni*. This is not the same word used when speaking about Yahuah. When the Bible addresses Yahuah directly, the word is *Adonai* — a form reserved for the Almighty. But *adoni* is the everyday word for “my master” or “my lord.” It is used all over the Old Testament for human kings, military commanders, husbands, and other authority figures.

Here are some clear examples. In **Genesis 23:6**, the Hittites call Abraham “my lord” — the same Hebrew word. Nobody claims Abraham was a divine person. In **1 Samuel 24:8**, David bows to King Saul and calls him “my lord the king.” Same word. In **1 Kings 1:17**, Bathsheba addresses King David as “my lord, O king.” Same word again. None of these people are Yahuah. The Hebrew itself is telling us: the second figure in Psalm 110:1 is a human lord — a king — not a second divine person.

**Pay close attention to what this means.** If the Holy Spirit wanted readers to understand the second figure as Yahuah, the text would have used *Adonai*. It did not. It used the common, everyday word for a human superior. The Hebrew made a deliberate choice, and that choice rules out the Trinitarian claim at the very foundation.

## 2.2 — The Type of Psalm — Royal Enthronement, Not a Statement About Who Yahuah Is

Psalm 110 belongs to a well-known category of psalms called royal enthronement psalms. These are poems written for the crowning or installation of a king in Israel. They use grand, exalted language to describe the king’s authority — but that authority always comes from Yahuah. The king is never presented as equal to Yahuah. He is always presented as Yahuah’s chosen agent.

Look at the pattern across these psalms. In **Psalm 2:6–7**, Yahuah says, “I have set my king on my holy hill of Zion,” and then declares the king to be His “son.” That is installation language. The king does not install himself. Yahuah installs him. In **2 Samuel 7:12–14**, Yahuah promises David that his offspring will sit on his throne, and Yahuah will be a father to him. Again — the ruler receives his position from Yahuah. In **Psalm 89:20–27**, the anointed king is exalted above other kings, but the text is crystal clear: Yahuah is the one doing the exalting. The king is still under Yahuah’s authority.

Psalm 110 fits this pattern exactly. Yahuah speaks. The appointed ruler receives a seat at the right hand. Yahuah subdues the enemies. Yahuah grants a priestly role. Every action in this psalm flows from Yahuah downward to the appointed king. That is delegation, not equality.

## 2.3 — The Priestly Order of Melchizedek

Psalm 110:4 adds another layer that actually works against the Trinitarian reading:

*"The LORD hath sworn, and will not repent, Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek." — Psalm 110:4 (KJV)*

Notice who is speaking and who is receiving. Yahuah swears an oath. The appointed ruler receives a priesthood. A priest, by definition, serves as a go-between — someone who stands between the people and Yahuah. If the second figure in Psalm 110 were Yahuah Himself, He would be appointing Himself as a priest to Himself. That makes no sense. The whole concept of priesthood requires two separate parties: the one being served (Yahuah) and the one doing the serving (the priest). The book of Hebrews confirms this. **Hebrews 5:5–6** says the Messiah “did not glorify himself” to become a high priest — Yahuah appointed him. That is the language of a servant receiving a role, not a co-equal divine person.

## 2.4 — Psalm 110:1b — “Until I Make Your Enemies Your Footstool”

The second half of verse 1 is easy to overlook, but it delivers a powerful point. Yahuah says the appointed ruler must **sit and wait** until Yahuah Himself subdues the enemies. The ruler is not doing the subduing. Yahuah is. The ruler’s job is to sit in the honored position and wait for Yahuah to finish the work.

This is confirmed in **1 Corinthians 15:25–28**, where Paul explains the same picture. The Messiah reigns until all enemies are put under his feet, and then — pay close attention — the Son himself is subjected to Yahuah, “that God may be all in all.” If the Messiah were co-equal with Yahuah, there would be no point where he hands authority back. But Paul says he does. That tells you everything you need to know about who holds ultimate authority.

PART THREE

**Key Verse Deep Dive**

## 3.1 — How Yahushua Used Psalm 110:1 in Matthew 22

In **Matthew 22:41–46**, Yahushua (Jesus) asks the Pharisees a question about this very psalm. He says: if the Messiah is David’s son, then why does David call him “my lord”? The Pharisees could not answer, and the conversation stopped.

Trinitarians jump on this and say, “See! The Messiah must be divine, because David would not call a mere human ‘my lord.’” But that is not what Yahushua was proving. His point was about **rank**, not about nature. David’s descendants were normally considered lesser than David himself. But this descendant — the Messiah — holds a rank so high that David calls him “my lord.” That is because Yahuah has exalted the Messiah above every other king. It does not mean the Messiah is Yahuah.

Here is a modern way to think about it. A grandfather might have a grandson who becomes the president of a country. The grandson now holds a rank higher than anything the grandfather ever held. The grandfather might say, “That is my lord the president.” That does not make the grandson the grandfather. It just means the grandson’s position is higher. That is exactly what Psalm 110:1 describes. Yahuah elevated the Messiah to the highest seat of delegated authority. David recognizes that rank.

## 3.2 — Peter’s Verdict in Acts 2:34–36 — The Apostolic Interpretation

If there is one passage that should end this debate forever, it is **Acts 2:34–36**. On the day of Pentecost, the apostle Peter stands up, quotes Psalm 110:1, and gives the inspired, apostolic conclusion:

*"For David is not ascended into the heavens: but he saith himself, The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou on my right hand, Until I make thy foes thy footstool. Therefore let all the house of Israel know assuredly, that God hath made that same Jesus, whom ye have crucified, both Lord and Christ." — Acts 2:34–36 (KJV)*

Read Peter’s conclusion carefully: Yahuah **made** Yahushua both Lord and Messiah. That word “made” is fatal to the Trinitarian argument. If Yahushua were eternally and inherently “Lord” as a co-equal divine person, there would be nothing for Yahuah to “make.” You do not “make” someone what they already are by nature. Peter is saying that the lordship and messiahship of Yahushua is something Yahuah **conferred** on him — something Yahuah granted after the resurrection and exaltation. That is appointment language. That is agency language. That is the opposite of “two persons who are equally Yahuah.”

**Acts 5:31** reinforces the same point: “Him hath God exalted with his right hand to be a Prince and a Saviour.” Yahuah exalted him. **Philippians 2:9–11** says the same: “Wherefore God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name.” In every case, the pattern is the same — Yahuah is the one acting, and the Messiah is the one receiving.

## 3.3 — The “Right Hand” — What It Actually Means in the Bible

Trinitarians often treat “sitting at the right hand of God” as if it proves equality with Yahuah. But the right hand in Scripture is always about **delegated authority and honor** — not about being the same person or the same God.

**Hebrews 1:3–4** says Yahushua sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high *after* he had purged sins. He was also “made so much better than the angels.” Notice: he was **made** better. That is not the language of someone who is inherently and eternally equal with Yahuah. **Ephesians 1:20–22** confirms the same: Yahuah set him at His right hand and placed all things under his feet. The action belongs to Yahuah. The position belongs to the Messiah because Yahuah put him there.

Here is the test that breaks the Trinitarian reading. **1 Corinthians 15:27–28** says that all things are put under the Messiah’s feet, but **Yahuah is the clear exception**. Paul goes out of his way to say, “It is manifest that he is excepted, which did put all things under him.” Then he adds: “And when all things shall be subdued unto him, then shall the Son also himself be subject unto him that put all things under him, that God may be all in all.” If the Messiah were Yahuah, he could not be subject to Yahuah. But Paul says he will be. The entire framework is delegation, not equality.

PART FOUR

**Supporting Evidence and Broader Patterns**

## 4.1 — The “Two Lords” Trap — Why It Does Not Create Two Yahuahs

Trinitarian reasoning often slides from “two persons” to “two divine persons” without proving the second step. Yes, Psalm 110:1 shows two figures. But notice how it identifies them. The first is **Yahuah** — the covenant name of the one true God. The second is *adoni* — a human lord who receives a seat, a kingdom, and a priesthood. The verse itself already gives you the categories. One is the Giver. The other is the receiver. There is no reason — and no textual support — for promoting the receiver to the same level as the Giver.

**Here is a consistency test that exposes the problem.** All through the Old Testament, Yahuah speaks through messengers, prophets, and angels using first-person language. If every time Yahuah speaks through an agent it creates a “second divine person,” then you would have dozens of divine persons scattered across Scripture. The angel in Exodus 3 speaks as Yahuah from the burning bush. The angel in Genesis 22 stops Abraham’s hand and speaks as Yahuah. The angel in Judges 6 sits under a tree and speaks as Yahuah. None of these are “a second Yahuah.” They are agents carrying Yahuah’s authority and speaking on His behalf. This is the same pattern. One Yahuah. One appointed agent. No confusion needed.

## 4.2 — The Septuagint and Early Jewish Reading

The Septuagint — the Greek translation of the Old Testament used by the early believers and quoted throughout the New Testament — translates Psalm 110:1 with two different Greek words. The first “Lord” is *Kurios* (standing in for Yahuah). The second is also *kurios*, but the context makes it clear that the second lord is the one being addressed, not the one doing the addressing. In early Jewish reading, this verse was always understood as Yahuah speaking to the Messiah — a human king whom Yahuah would exalt. The idea that it described two persons within one God was foreign to every Jewish reader before the rise of later Trinitarian theology.

## 4.3 — Luke 1:32–33 and Acts 13:22–23 — The Davidic Frame

The angel’s announcement to Mary in **Luke 1:32–33** places the Messiah squarely in the Davidic line: “He shall be great, and shall be called the Son of the Highest: and the Lord God shall give unto him the throne of his father David.” Notice: Yahuah **gives** the throne. The Messiah does not claim it by inherent right as a co-equal God. He receives it as the promised descendant of David.

Paul says the same in **Acts 13:22–23**: “Of this man’s seed hath God according to his promise raised unto Israel a Saviour, Jesus.” The Messiah comes from David’s line. He is raised up by Yahuah according to a promise. That is the fulfillment of a covenant, not the unveiling of a secret second divine person.

## 4.4 — Hebrews 1:1–2 — The Son Speaks in the Last Days, Not in the Old Testament

The opening of Hebrews provides an important control point. **Hebrews 1:1–2** says: “God, who at sundry times and in divers manners spake in time past unto the fathers by the prophets, hath in these last days spoken unto us by his Son.” Notice the contrast. In the Old Testament period, Yahuah spoke through prophets. In the last days, He speaks through His Son. The Son is the voice of the new era. He was not the speaker in the psalms. Yahuah was. This means Psalm 110:1 is Yahuah speaking about the future Messiah — not a conversation between two co-equal divine persons who have always existed together.

PART FIVE

**Summary and Conclusion**

## 5.1 — What the Text Actually Says

Psalm 110:1 is a royal enthronement psalm. Yahuah — the one true God — speaks to the Davidic king (ultimately the Messiah) and tells him to sit at His right hand until all enemies are subdued. The Hebrew word for the second “lord” is *adoni*, the common word for a human superior, not *Adonai*, the word reserved for Yahuah. The verse grants the Messiah the highest seat of delegated authority in the universe. It proves that Yahuah and the Messiah are two distinct persons, but it does not prove they are the same God or share a single divine nature. Every New Testament use of this verse confirms the same reading: Yahuah exalts, the Messiah is exalted. One acts, the other receives.

## 5.2 — What the Trinitarian Reading Requires

To make Psalm 110:1 support the Trinity, a reader must import several ideas that are not found in the text. They must ignore the distinction between *adoni* and *Adonai* — the very distinction that the Hebrew text makes. They must treat “sitting at the right hand” as equality rather than delegated authority, even though every New Testament explanation treats it as delegation. They must explain away Acts 2:36, where Peter says Yahuah **made** Yahushua Lord and Messiah — language that proves his lordship was conferred, not inherent. They must explain away 1 Corinthians 15:28, where Paul says the Son will be subjected to Yahuah at the end. They must ignore the entire royal enthronement genre of the psalm. And they must read a multi-person Godhead into a passage that no Jewish reader — including Yahushua himself and the apostles — ever read that way.

## 5.3 — Conclusion

Psalm 110:1 does not teach the Trinity. It teaches that Yahuah, the one and only God, appoints, exalts, and empowers His chosen Messiah. The Messiah receives a seat at the highest position of delegated authority. He receives a kingdom. He receives a priesthood. Everything in this psalm flows in one direction: from Yahuah to His anointed king. The same God who declared in **Deuteronomy 6:4**, “Hear, O Israel: Yahuah our God, Yahuah is one,” is the same God who speaks in Psalm 110:1, installing one Messiah on one throne under one authority. There is one God, one Lord appointed by that God, and one direction of power — from the Father to the Son. That is not the Trinity. That is the gospel.

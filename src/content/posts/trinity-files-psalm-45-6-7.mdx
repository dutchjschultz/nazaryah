**Psalm 45:6-7**

**A Throne, and a God Above It**

*The king hailed as god still bows to a God of his own*

A king with a God above him is no God --- and this king has one.

--- The Standing Stone ---

> **Behind "LORD" in your Bible lies a hidden name --- in the Hebrew it
> is Yahuah** *Psalm 83:18***; Yahuah is the Father** *Isaiah 63:16***;
> Yahuah is the only God, beside Him there is no other** *Isaiah
> 45:5***; therefore Yahuah the Father is the only true God, leaving no
> room for a second or third person** *1 Corinthians 8:6***.**

Part One

**The Claim and Its Problems**

***1 --- What the Trinitarian Reading Argues***

Psalm 45:6--7 is one of the most popular Old Testament verses pressed
into service for the Trinity. The whole argument leans on one phrase:
"Thy throne, O God, is for ever and ever." The claim is that the
psalmist calls someone "God" directly. And because the book of Hebrews
later quotes this verse and applies it to the Son *Hebrews 1:8*, it is
taken as proof that the Messiah is Yahuah Himself --- a second co-equal,
co-eternal person of a triune Godhead.

Put plainly, the argument runs like this: the Father calls the Son
"God," so the Son must be God in the same way the Father is God. Stack
that beside *Hebrews 1:8*, and the case looks settled. It is not.

***2 --- The Problems Before We Open the Text***

Before we examine a single Hebrew word, the reading already carries
serious problems.

First, everyone agrees --- including the scholars who hold to the
Trinity --- that Psalm 45 is a royal wedding song. The sons of Korah
wrote it for a Davidic king on his wedding day. The heading calls it a
shir yedidoth, a "song of loves." This is not a lesson about the nature
of God. It is a wedding hymn for a man who wears a crown.

Second, consider what the reading actually demands. It would mean that
Yahuah --- the Almighty Creator of heaven and earth --- is the one being
described as desiring a woman's beauty *Psalm 45:11*, surrounded by
"honourable women" *Psalm 45:9*, and having a bride led to him in
embroidered garments *Psalm 45:13--15*. That is not language anyone
should lay on the Almighty. It fits a human king celebrating his
marriage and nothing else.

Third, the claim is often that the Father is speaking here, addressing
the Son as "God." But that is not what the text says. The speaker is the
psalmist --- a human poet --- and he tells us so in the opening line:
"My heart is inditing a good matter: I speak of the things which I have
made touching the king" *Psalm 45:1*. This is a man writing a song about
a king. It is not a conversation between members of a Godhead.

Part Two

**Walking Through Psalm 45**

***3 --- The Setting: A Royal Wedding Song***

The Psalm opens with the poet saying his heart is bursting with a good
theme, his tongue "like the pen of a ready writer." He is writing to the
king. From the first breath the subject is an earthly ruler. Verse 2
praises him as "fairer than the children of men" and says favor is
poured into his lips. Verse 3 tells him to strap his sword to his thigh.
Verse 5 describes his sharp arrows piercing the hearts of his enemies.
Every line is the portrait of a human warrior-king --- not the Almighty.

***4 --- The Disputed Verse***

> **Psalm 45:6**
>
> *Thy throne, O God, is for ever and ever: the sceptre of thy kingdom
> is a right sceptre.*

This is the one verse the whole argument depends on. The Hebrew reads
kis'akha Elohim olam va'ed. The key word is Elohim. The claim is that
this must be direct address --- "Your throne, O God." But the phrase has
at least five honest renderings: (1) "Your throne, O God, is forever"
--- the king is called "God"; (2) "Your throne is God forever" ---
Yahuah is the foundation of the king's authority; (3) "Your divine
throne is forever" --- the throne is God-given; (4) "God is your throne
forever" --- Yahuah is the source of the king's power; (5) "Your throne
is like God's throne, forever" --- a comparison.

These are not invented alternatives. The NRSV margin reads "Your throne
is a throne of God." The New Jerusalem Bible reads "Your throne is from
God." The TEV reads "The kingdom that God has given you." The REB reads
"God has enthroned you."

This kind of construction is common in Hebrew --- two nouns set side by
side with an implied "is" between them. Psalm 73:26 does the very same
thing: chelqi Elohim means "my portion is God." By that pattern,
kis'akha Elohim can simply mean "your throne is God" --- Yahuah is the
power behind the king's throne. The king rules because Yahuah set him
there, not because the king is Yahuah.

***5 --- The Verse That Destroys the Claim***

> **Psalm 45:7**
>
> *Thou lovest righteousness, and hatest wickedness: therefore God, thy
> God, hath anointed thee with the oil of gladness above thy fellows.*

Even if you grant the "O God" reading of verse 6, verse 7 makes the
conclusion impossible. Three things in this single verse undo the claim.

**First, the king has a God over him.** The text says "God, thy God,
hath anointed thee." If this king were Yahuah, He would not have a God.
The Almighty answers to no one. He has no superior who anoints Him. This
only works if the king is a human being under the authority of the one
true God.

**Second, he was anointed because he loved righteousness.** Notice the
word "therefore." It names a reason: because this king loved what was
right and hated what was wrong, therefore Yahuah anointed him. But
Yahuah earns nothing. He is righteous by His own nature. You do not
reward God for choosing to be righteous. That language fits only a human
being --- a king who made the right choice and was blessed for it.

**Third, he has "fellows" --- companions and equals.** The Hebrew chaber
means those who stand close to you, your peers. The king was anointed
"above" his fellows --- lifted up from among those who are like him. But
Yahuah has no peers; no one is like Him. "To whom then will ye liken me,
or shall I be equal? saith the Holy One" *Isaiah 40:25*. The language of
"fellows" fits only a human king among other human rulers.

***6 --- The Wedding Context: Why This Cannot Describe Yahuah***

The verses right after the disputed line make the reading harder still.
Verse 9 sets "king's daughters among thy honourable women" and places
the queen at the king's right hand in gold of Ophir. Verses 10--11 tell
the bride to forget her own people, "so shall the king greatly desire
thy beauty." Verses 13--15 describe the royal bride led to the king in
embroidered garments, her companions following her into the palace.

Sit with that. The Psalm describes a king physically drawn to his bride
and surrounded by women. Would Yahuah "greatly desire the beauty" of a
woman? Would He be pictured with "honourable women" about Him? Applied
to the Creator the words are an insult. Applied to a human king on his
wedding day they fit exactly.

***7 --- What the Hebrew Actually Allows***

Psalm 45:6 is written so that more than one faithful translation stands
open. That is not a flaw in Scripture; it is simply how Hebrew sometimes
works. The real question is what Elohim is doing here --- a direct
address ("O God"), a title pointing to God-given authority ("your throne
is God's"), or elevated language for a king who reigns on God's behalf.
Translators have weighed this for centuries.

But here is the hinge: whatever rendering you choose, it must agree with
the very next verse. Verse 7 says "God, thy God, hath anointed thee."
That line locks the meaning into a single channel. The king is honored,
celebrated, enthroned --- and still under Yahuah, still anointed by
Yahuah. So verse 6 cannot be made to prove the king is Yahuah.

Even the careful academic surveys admit the looseness of the grammar.
Murray J. Harris, writing in the Tyndale Bulletin, lays out the main
translation options for Elohim in these verses and shows why the phrase
must be handled with care. The debate among scholars is real --- and
that is the point: a doctrine of God Almighty cannot be built on a line
its own defenders concede is ambiguous. Context decides, and the
controlling context is the anointing in verse 7.

***8 --- The Messiah Has a God***

Psalm 45:7 is no isolated remark. The New Testament says the same thing
again and again: even after his resurrection and exaltation, the Messiah
still has a God over him. That confirms the plain reading --- Psalm 45
praises an anointed king who serves under Yahuah, not a second person
who is Yahuah by nature.

Weigh the witnesses. Yahushua (Jesus) says, "I ascend unto my Father,
and your Father; and to my God, and your God" *John 20:17*. The risen
and glorified Yahushua names "my God" four times in one verse
*Revelation 3:12*. Paul calls the Father "the God of our Lord Yahushua"
*Ephesians 1:17*. Peter declares that "God hath made that same Yahushua
both Lord and Messiah" *Acts 2:36*. And Paul says that when all things
are put under the Son, even then the Son himself will be "subject unto"
the One who gave him that authority *1 Corinthians 15:27--28*.

The pattern is unmistakable. From his earthly ministry to his glorified
reign, Yahushua always has a God over him. Psalm 45:7 drops into that
pattern without a seam. It does not prove the Messiah is God. It proves
he is under God.

Part Three

**The Word Elohim**

***9 --- Elohim Does Not Always Mean Yahuah Almighty***

The whole case for this verse depends on reading Elohim as a reference
to Yahuah Almighty. But Elohim is not a personal name. It is a title
with a range of meanings. Most often it speaks of Yahuah. Yet it is also
laid on human judges and rulers. Exodus 21:6 brings a servant before
ha-Elohim --- the judges. Exodus 22:8--9 uses Elohim for the judges who
settle disputes. And in Psalm 82:6 Yahuah Himself says, "I have said, Ye
are gods \[Elohim\]; and all of you are children of the most High."

Yahushua used this very argument when men accused him of blasphemy *John
10:34--35*. His point was simple: Scripture calls human beings Elohim
when they act as Yahuah's representatives. If the title fell on judges
without anyone thinking those judges were Yahuah, then the same title on
a Davidic king in a wedding psalm proves nothing about the king being
Yahuah either.

***10 --- The 1 Chronicles Parallel Settles the Question***

Here is perhaps the strongest piece on the table. The language of Psalm
45 matches the coronation of Solomon almost word for word.

> **1 Chronicles 28:5**
>
> *And of all my sons, (for the LORD \[Yahuah\] hath given me many
> sons,) he hath chosen Solomon my son to sit upon the throne of the
> kingdom of the LORD \[Yahuah\] over Israel.*
>
> **1 Chronicles 29:23**
>
> *Then Solomon sat on the throne of the LORD \[Yahuah\] as king instead
> of David his father.*

Notice what that tells us. The throne in Psalm 45:6 was never the king's
own. It is Yahuah's throne, and the Davidic king sits on it as a
steward. So when the psalmist says "Thy throne, Elohim, is forever and
ever," he means the king's authority rests on Yahuah's eternal throne.
The king sits on God's throne. The throne belongs to God. The king is
not God.

Ask a plain question. If the throne was never the king's to begin with
--- if it always belonged to Yahuah and the king was merely permitted to
sit on it --- why would the psalmist suddenly announce that the king is
the God who owns the throne? He would not. He is saying the opposite:
the king's throne endures because Yahuah stands behind it.

Part Four

**The Broader Pattern**

***11 --- The Principle of Divine Agency***

All through the Hebrew Scriptures, Yahuah's chosen representatives are
described with language that reflects the authority of the One who sent
them. Moses was told he would be "as God" (Elohim) to Pharaoh *Exodus
7:1*. The Angel of Yahuah speaks as though he is Yahuah *Genesis
22:11--12* *Exodus 3:2--6*, yet he remains a messenger. The judges of
Israel were called Elohim because they carried out Yahuah's justice. In
every case the title points to the authority behind the person, not to
who the person actually is.

The Davidic king fits the same mold. He sits on Yahuah's throne, rules
Yahuah's people, and enforces Yahuah's justice. He can be called Elohim
the same way Moses and the judges were --- not because the king is
Yahuah, but because the king carries Yahuah's rule on earth.

***12 --- Hebrews 1:8 Does Not Change the Meaning***

The last refuge is almost always Hebrews 1:8, where the writer quotes
Psalm 45:6--7 and applies it to the Son. A full treatment of Hebrews 1:8
belongs to its own study, but a few points must be set down here.

The writer of Hebrews was reading the Greek translation of the Hebrew
Scriptures. That Greek is one rendering of the Hebrew text, and its
choices do not overrule what the original Hebrew means. Beyond that,
Hebrews quotes many passages first spoken of other people. Psalm 2 was a
coronation psalm; 2 Samuel 7:14 spoke of Solomon; Psalm 102 was
addressed to Yahuah. In each the writer draws a typological line --- a
pattern begun in an earlier figure and brought to its fullness in
Yahushua. The quotation of Psalm 45 works the same way. It marks
Yahushua as the ultimate Davidic king who sits on Yahuah's throne. It
does not turn him into Yahuah.

And here is the detail that should end the appeal: the writer of Hebrews
quotes verse 7 too. He keeps the words "Therefore God, even thy God,
hath anointed thee" *Hebrews 1:9*. He does not trim the line that gives
the Son a God above him; he leaves it standing. If he believed the Son
was Yahuah Almighty, why preserve a verse that says the Son has a God
who anointed him? The only honest answer is that he understood Yahushua
as the exalted Messiah at Yahuah's right hand --- not as Yahuah Himself.

***13 --- The Pattern of Anointing Proves Subordination***

Anointing in Scripture moves one direction only: the greater anoints the
lesser. Samuel anointed Saul and David because Yahuah told him to.
Yahuah anointed Yahushua with the Holy Spirit and power *Acts 10:38*.
The one who anoints always holds authority over the one anointed.

In Psalm 45:7 the king is anointed by Yahuah --- his God. That is the
language of commission and appointment, not of co-equality. You do not
anoint your equal. You anoint your chosen servant for a task.

Part Five

**Standing the Sources in Daylight**

***14 --- Scholarly Resources***

These sources are not the authority --- Scripture is. They are set here
only to show that the translation questions raised above are openly
discussed in serious academic work, and that even careful defenders of
the doctrine concede the Hebrew of verse 6 is genuinely uncertain.

**Murray J. Harris** --- "The Translation of ʾElohim in Psalm 45:7--8,"
Tyndale Bulletin. Surveys the grammatically possible renderings and
shows that context, not the bare phrase, must decide.

**Richard D. Patterson** --- "A Multiplex Approach to Psalm 45," Grace
Theological Journal. Analyzes the psalm's form and shows how the wedding
and kingship themes govern the disputed lines.

45:6--7 in Hebrews 1:8--9, University of Pretoria. Examines how Hebrews
uses royal-psalm language to honor the exalted Son while keeping verse 9
intact --- "God, thy God, hath anointed thee."

Conclusion

**The Verdict**

Psalm 45 is a wedding song. From the first verse to the last it
celebrates an anointed king --- his favor, his sword, his bride, his
palace --- and at its center it grounds his throne in the eternal throne
of Yahuah. Read whole, it never once lifts the king to the place of the
One who anointed him.

The testimony of Moses stands unbroken: Shema Yisra'el, Yahuah Eloheinu,
Yahuah Echad --- "Hear, O Israel: Yahuah our God, Yahuah is one"
(Deuteronomy 6:4). The king on that throne is the servant who received
the oil; Yahuah is the One who poured it.

> **If the very next verse says "God, thy God, hath anointed thee above
> thy fellows," then the king on that throne is not the God who anointed
> him --- he is the servant who received the oil.**

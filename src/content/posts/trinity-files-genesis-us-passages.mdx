Chapter 1

**“Like One of Us”**

*A Rebuttal of the Trinitarian Reading of Genesis 1:26, 3:22, and 11:7*

An Examination of Hebrew Plural Speech, the Divine Council, and What “Us” Actually Means

**Trinitarian Argument Strength:** ★★☆☆☆  2 out of 5

*The plural “us” is real in the Hebrew grammar, but the text never identifies who is included in that plural. Three well-established readings fit the grammar without requiring a Trinity.*

Part One

**Framing the Problem**

**1.1 — What Trinitarians Claim**

There are three places in the book of Genesis where Yahuah (God) uses the word “us” or “our” when speaking. Trinitarians treat these three passages as a cluster. They argue that the plural language proves there is more than one person inside Yahuah. Here are the three verses they point to.

**First, Genesis 1:26:** “Let us make man in our image, after our likeness.” Second, **Genesis 3:22:** “Behold, the man is become as one of us, to know good and evil.” Third, **Genesis 11:7:** “Let us go down, and there confound their language.”

The Trinitarian argument is simple. They say: “Yahuah is speaking. He says ‘us.’ Therefore there must be more than one person inside Yahuah.” They then connect these verses to later Trinitarian doctrine and argue that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are all present in these conversations.

**1.2 — The Problem Before We Even Open the Text**

Here is the first problem. Not one of these three verses names who the “us” is. **Not one.** The text says “us” but never says “Father, Son, and Spirit.” That identification has to be imported from outside Genesis. The Hebrew grammar confirms there is a plural. It does not confirm there is a Trinity.

Think of it this way. If a king says to his court, “Let us go to war,” the word “us” proves the king is speaking to others. It does not prove those others are all kings. It does not even prove they are the same kind of being. The plural tells you someone else is present. It does not tell you who that someone is.

Even many careful Trinitarian scholars admit this. They do not treat these verses as standalone proofs of the Trinity. Instead, they call them “hints” or “compatible with later revelation.” That is an honest admission that the text itself does not make the case.

**1.3 — The Hebrews 1:1–2 Control Point**

Hebrews 1:1–2 says that Yahuah spoke through prophets in the past, but “in these last days” He spoke through His Son. This means the Son was not the one speaking in Genesis. If the Son was already speaking as Yahuah in Genesis 1, then there is no “new” revelation in the last days. Hebrews makes a time distinction: prophets first, Son later. That distinction collapses if the Son was already active as one of the “us” in Genesis.

Part Two

**Verse-by-Verse Examination**

**2.1 — Genesis 1:26–27: “Let Us Make Man in Our Image”**

*“And God said, Let us make man in our image, after our likeness: and let them have dominion over the fish of the sea, and over the fowl of the air, and over the cattle, and over all the earth, and over every creeping thing that creepeth upon the earth. So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him; male and female created he them.” — Genesis 1:26–27 (KJV)*

Pay close attention to what happens between verse 26 and verse 27. **In verse 26, the language is plural: “Let us make” and “our image.” But in verse 27, the language switches to singular: “So God created man in his own image.”** Not “their” image. Not “our” image. *His own* image.

This switch is critical. If the “us” in verse 26 meant co-equal persons of a Trinity, you would expect verse 27 to say “They created man in their image.” But it does not. The actual creation is attributed to Yahuah alone, in the singular. The plural language in verse 26 looks like an announcement made in the presence of a court, while the actual act belongs to one Yahuah.

This pattern matches how kings speak. A king says “Let us go to war” in the presence of his court. Then the text says “the king went to war.” The announcement is plural. The authority is singular. That is exactly what Genesis 1:26–27 does.

**2.2 — Genesis 3:22: “The Man Has Become Like One of Us”**

*“And the LORD God said, Behold, the man is become as one of us, to know good and evil: and now, lest he put forth his hand, and take also of the tree of life, and eat, and live for ever.” — Genesis 3:22 (KJV)*

Notice what the verse says and what it does not say. Yahuah says the man has become “like one of us.” The comparison is about knowing good and evil. It is about moral discernment. It is not about the man becoming equal to Yahuah in nature or power.

Now think about the phrase “one of us.” This phrase makes the most natural sense if Yahuah is speaking in the presence of a group — a heavenly court or council. The man has crossed a line. He now possesses something that belongs to the realm above him. Yahuah announces this to the beings in His presence.

If the “us” were the Trinity, the verse would be saying: “The man has become like one of the three persons of the Godhead in knowing good and evil.” But that creates an odd picture. Did the man become like the Father? Like the Son? Like the Spirit? The Trinity reading creates a category problem that the heavenly-court reading does not.

**2.3 — Genesis 11:7: “Let Us Go Down and Confuse Their Language”**

*“Go to, let us go down, and there confound their language, that they may not understand one another’s speech.” — Genesis 11:7 (KJV)*

This verse describes Yahuah responding to the Tower of Babel. The people have gathered to build a tower reaching to heaven. Yahuah says, “Let us go down.”

This is court language. It is the language of a king who has assessed a situation and now announces his decision to act. The “us” fits naturally as Yahuah speaking to His heavenly agents — the angels who carry out divine judgments throughout Scripture.

But here is the key. Just like Genesis 1, the very next verse switches to singular. **Genesis 11:8 says: “So the LORD scattered them abroad.”** Not “they scattered.” The announcement was plural. The action was singular. The pattern is the same in all three passages: Yahuah deliberates in the presence of others, then Yahuah alone acts.

Part Three

**What the Hebrew Meant to Its Hearers**

**3.1 — The Hebrew Words Are Genuinely Plural**

Before we go further, the Hebrew grammar needs to be addressed directly. In all three passages, the plural is real. It is not a translation error and it is not a quirk of English.

In Genesis 1:26, the verb *naʿaśeh* (“let us make”) is first-person plural. The suffixes on “image” and “likeness” (*-nû*, meaning “our”) are also plural. In Genesis 3:22, the phrase *kəʾaḥad mimmennu* means “like one from us” — the suffix *-nu* is first-person plural. In Genesis 11:7, both verbs *nērədāh* (“let us go down”) and *wənāḇəlāh* (“let us confuse”) are first-person plural.

So the grammar is not in dispute. The plural is real. The question is: **who is included in that plural?** And on that question, the grammar is silent. It does not name Father, Son, and Spirit. It does not name anyone at all.

**3.2 — What an Ancient Hebrew Hearer Would Have Understood**

An ancient Hebrew listener would have heard this plural language and immediately thought of the heavenly court. This is not guesswork. The concept of Yahuah sitting among His heavenly council is one of the most well-established ideas in the Hebrew Bible.

Psalm 89:7 says Yahuah is “greatly to be feared in the assembly of the holy ones.” 1 Kings 22:19 shows Yahuah on His throne with the host of heaven around Him. Job 1:6 and 2:1 describe the “sons of God” presenting themselves before Yahuah. Daniel 7:10 says “ten thousand times ten thousand stood before Him.”

This is the world the Hebrew reader lived in. When they heard Yahuah say “Let us,” they pictured a throne room — a king addressing His court before He acts. They did not picture three co-equal persons debating among themselves. That concept did not exist in Hebrew thought until centuries after these texts were written.

Here is a modern way to think about it. When a CEO says “Let’s launch the product,” the word “let’s” includes the team in the room. Nobody concludes that the CEO is three people. The CEO is one person addressing the group, and the CEO alone has the authority to launch. That is exactly the pattern in Genesis: plural announcement, singular action.

Part Four

**Supporting Evidence**

**4.1 — The Divine Council in Scripture**

The idea that Yahuah speaks in the presence of a heavenly court is not a fringe theory. It is woven throughout the Hebrew Bible. But one thing must be perfectly clear before we look at these passages: **Yahuah does not take counsel from anyone.** He does not ask for advice. He does not need input. Everything Yahuah does flows from His own righteousness. He cannot go in any direction other than who He is.

So when Scripture shows Yahuah speaking in the presence of His heavenly court, the picture is a king issuing commands — not a committee taking votes. Yahuah announces His will. The angels receive their assignments. That is the relationship.

Consider 1 Kings 22:19–22. The prophet Micaiah sees Yahuah sitting on His throne with the entire host of heaven standing on His right and left. Yahuah declares what He intends to do regarding Ahab. A spirit comes forward and offers to carry out the task. **Yahuah does not ask the spirit for a plan. Yahuah does not weigh options. He already knows the outcome.** He commissions the spirit to go and act. This is a throne-room scene where the King sends His agents. It is not a boardroom meeting where equals brainstorm together.

In Job chapters 1 and 2, the “sons of God” come to present themselves before Yahuah. The adversary comes among them. Yahuah speaks with the adversary and sets the boundaries for what he may do with Job. Again, Yahuah is the one setting the terms. He is not receiving advice.

In Psalm 82:1, Yahuah “stands in the congregation of the mighty” and “judges among the gods.” These “gods” are not rival deities. They are members of the heavenly assembly. Yahuah is the judge. They are accountable to Him.

This is the world of the text. When Yahuah says “Let us” in Genesis, the picture is a sovereign king announcing His decision in the presence of His servants. He is not consulting them. He is including them in the announcement because they will carry out assignments under His authority.

**4.2 — The Singular-Plural Pattern Across All Three Passages**

One of the strongest arguments against the Trinitarian reading is the pattern that repeats in all three Genesis passages. Watch what happens.

**Genesis 1:26 says “Let us make.” Genesis 1:27 says “God created.”** Plural announcement, singular action.

**Genesis 3:22 says “one of us.” Genesis 3:23 says “the LORD God sent him.”** Plural reference, singular action.

**Genesis 11:7 says “Let us go down.” Genesis 11:8 says “the LORD scattered them.”** Plural announcement, singular action.

Every single time, the follow-up verse reverts to the singular. If these were three co-equal persons acting together, the text should say “they created,” “they sent him,” “they scattered.” But it never does. The pattern is consistent: Yahuah announces in the presence of His servants, then Yahuah alone performs the act. This matches a king on His throne. It does not match three persons who are equally Yahuah.

**4.3 — The LXX Comparison: What the Greek Translation Shows**

The Septuagint (the ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, often called the LXX) is important because it tells us how Jewish scholars translated these passages two to three centuries before Yahushua (Jesus) was born. These translators were not Trinitarians. They had no reason to insert plurality for theological purposes.

In Genesis 1:26, the LXX preserves the plural: *poiēsōmen* (“let us make”) and “our image.” In Genesis 3:22, the LXX also preserves the plural: *hōs heis ex hēmōn* (“as one of us”). The Greek translators did not try to hide or smooth over the plural language. They translated it honestly. But they also did not interpret it as a Trinity. Jewish tradition consistently understood this as divine council language.

**4.4 — Isaiah 6:8: The Missing “Us” in the Greek**

This is where the Trinitarian “plural speech” argument gets interesting. Trinitarians often add a fourth verse to their cluster: Isaiah 6:8, where Yahuah says, “Who will go for us?”

In the Hebrew text, the plural is clear. The phrase is *u-mi yelek-lanu* — “and who will go for us?” The suffix *-lanu* is first-person plural: “for us.”

But here is the surprise. In the Septuagint, the most common Greek form of this verse does not have “for us” at all. Instead, it reads: “Who will go to this people?” The plural pronoun disappears entirely in the Greek translation.

Why does this matter? Because it shows that even the ancient translators did not treat the “us” as a theologically essential word. If they believed the “us” proved plurality within Yahuah, they would never have translated it out. The fact that the LXX drops it suggests the translators understood it as a heavenly-court address — important for the scene, but not a claim about Yahuah’s internal nature.

**4.5 — John 12:37–41: What “Isaiah Saw His Glory” Actually Means**

Some Trinitarians try to connect Isaiah 6 to the Messiah by pointing to John 12:41. This verse says that Isaiah “saw his glory and spoke of him.” They argue this means Isaiah’s throne-room vision in Isaiah 6 was actually a vision of the pre-incarnate Messiah sitting on the throne. If that were true, the “us” in Isaiah 6:8 might include the Son.

But this argument falls apart when you slow down and look at what John is actually doing in this passage. Let’s walk through it step by step.

In John 12:37, John notes that the people did not believe in Yahushua even though he performed many signs. John wants to explain why. So he reaches back to Isaiah for an answer.

**In John 12:38, he quotes Isaiah 53:1:** “Who has believed our report?” This is from the Suffering Servant passage. It is about the Messiah being rejected. John is saying: “Isaiah predicted this rejection.”

**In John 12:40, he quotes Isaiah 6:10:** “He has blinded their eyes and hardened their heart.” This is from Isaiah’s temple vision. It is about Yahuah allowing the people’s hearts to become dull so they cannot see or repent. John is saying: “Isaiah also predicted that the people would be unable to respond.”

**Then in John 12:41, John wraps it up:** “Isaiah said these things because he saw his glory and spoke of him.”

Here is the key. The phrase “these things” points back to *both* Isaiah quotations — Isaiah 53 and Isaiah 6. John is not saying “Isaiah saw Yahushua physically sitting on the throne in Isaiah 6.” He is saying something much simpler: Isaiah, as a prophet, saw the Messiah’s story in advance. He saw the glory that would be revealed through Yahushua. He saw the rejection that would come. And he wrote about both.

Think of it this way. If someone says, “Martin Luther King saw the future of civil rights and spoke of it,” nobody thinks King literally time-traveled. They mean he had a prophetic vision of what was coming and announced it. That is what John means about Isaiah. Isaiah saw the glory of the Messiah’s mission and spoke about it through his prophecies.

There is one more detail worth noting. John’s quotation of Isaiah 6:10 in verse 40 closely follows the wording of the Septuagint — including the phrase “and I would heal them.” This is the Greek form, not a fresh translation from Hebrew. John is working in the same textual tradition as the LXX. And as we saw in section 4.4, that same LXX tradition dropped the “for us” from Isaiah 6:8. So even the Bible that John relied on did not preserve the plural in that verse. If the “us” were a Trinity proof text, John would have quoted it. He did not.

**4.6 — How the NT Uses Isaiah 6:9–10 Everywhere**

The hardening passage from Isaiah 6:9–10 is quoted multiple times in the New Testament. Each time, the writers follow the Septuagint wording rather than translating directly from Hebrew. Here is the pattern.

**Matthew 13:14–15** quotes Isaiah 6:9–10 at length. The wording aligns with the LXX’s descriptive framing (“this people’s heart became dull”) rather than the Hebrew imperative form (“Make the heart fat”).

**Acts 28:26–27** gives the longest and clearest quotation. Paul uses the LXX form to explain Jewish rejection of the gospel message. The wording tracks the Septuagint almost word for word.

**Mark 4:12 and Luke 8:10** compress the quotation into a shorter form. The relationship to the Hebrew versus the LXX is harder to pin down because the quotation is intentionally abbreviated.

The point is this. When the New Testament writers quoted Isaiah 6, they consistently worked from the Greek tradition — the same tradition that does not include “for us” in Isaiah 6:8. None of them used Isaiah 6:8’s “us” as evidence for the Trinity. Not once. If the “us” in Isaiah 6:8 were a Trinity proof text, you would expect at least one New Testament writer to point it out. None of them did.

**4.7 — The Hebrew Understanding of the Hardening Words**

While we are in Isaiah 6, it is worth noting what the Hebrew words in Isaiah 6:10 actually mean, because this passage is deeply connected to how the New Testament explains Israel’s response to the Messiah.

The Hebrew uses three body-part commands in a row. First, *hashmen* (from *shaman*), meaning “make fat.” A “fat heart” in Hebrew means a heart that has become insulated — it cannot feel. Second, *hakbed* (from *kabad*), meaning “make heavy.” “Heavy ears” are ears weighed down so they cannot hear. Third, *hasha* (from *sha’a*), meaning “smear shut” or “glaze over.” “Smeared eyes” are eyes filmed over so they cannot see.

In Hebrew thought, the heart is the center of understanding and will. The ears are the organ of covenant obedience (“hear” in Hebrew also means “obey”). The eyes are the organ of perception and discernment. So Isaiah 6:10 is covenant-court language: the people have resisted so long that Yahuah now sends truth that will confirm their chosen blindness. Fat heart, heavy ears, filmed eyes. This is judgment language, not Trinity language.

Part Five

**Summary and Conclusion**

**5.1 — What the Text Actually Says**

In three separate passages, the book of Genesis records Yahuah using plural speech: “Let us make,” “like one of us,” and “Let us go down.” The Hebrew grammar confirms that the plural is real. The verb forms and pronoun suffixes are genuinely first-person plural.

However, in every single case, the very next verse reverts to the singular. Yahuah alone creates. Yahuah alone sends Adam out. Yahuah alone scatters the nations. The consistent pattern is: plural announcement in the presence of a court, followed by singular action from the one true Yahuah. This matches the divine council pattern seen throughout the Hebrew Bible in passages like 1 Kings 22, Job 1–2, Psalm 82, Psalm 89, and Daniel 7.

The related verse Isaiah 6:8 (“Who will go for us?”) shows the same pattern in Hebrew, but the ancient Greek translation (the LXX) drops the plural entirely — confirming that even pre-Messiah Jewish translators did not treat the “us” as a theological statement about Yahuah’s inner nature.

**5.2 — What the Trinitarian Reading Requires**

To read the Trinity into these passages, the Trinitarian must assume the following.

They must assume the “us” refers to the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — even though the text never names them. They must assume the Son was active and speaking in Genesis — even though Hebrews 1:1–2 says the Son spoke only in “these last days.” They must ignore the singular-to-plural switch that happens in all three passages. They must override the well-established Hebrew concept of the divine council. They must assume the LXX translators and all pre-Messiah Jewish readers were wrong about what these passages meant. And they must explain why not a single New Testament writer ever quotes Genesis 1:26, 3:22, or 11:7 as evidence for the Trinity.

**5.3 — Conclusion**

The three “us” passages in Genesis are among the most frequently cited Trinitarian proof texts in the Torah. They are also among the weakest. The plural language is real, but the identification of that plural is not in the text. The divine council reading fits the Hebrew grammar, matches the immediate context, and is confirmed by every heavenly-court scene in the Hebrew Bible.

Yahuah is one. He speaks in the presence of His heavenly court. He announces His will to His servants. And then He alone acts. That is what Genesis says. That is what the Hebrew shows. That is what the pattern confirms.

**As Deuteronomy 6:4 declares: “Shma Yisrael, Yahuah Eloheinu, Yahuah Echad” — “Hear, O Israel: Yahuah our God, Yahuah is one.”**

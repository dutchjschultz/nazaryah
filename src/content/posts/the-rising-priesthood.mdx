*Eleazar, Ithamar, Zadok — and the Priesthood Yahuah Kept Raising*

•  •  •

Most believers know that Yahushua is our High Priest. Very few have followed the line that gets us there. It is not a straight line. It is a thousand years of a priesthood dying, being raised, defiling itself, being raised again — until at last it is not raised but replaced altogether. And one name marks the thread from beginning to end.

**1 · A Name Full of the Sanctuary**

The name is **Eleazar** (H499). It means *“El has helped.”* Trace every man in Scripture who carries it and a pattern rises off the page:

Eleazar son of Aaron — the high priest who received Aaron’s garments (Numbers 20).

Eleazar son of Abinadab — consecrated to guard the ark at Kiriath-jearim (1 Samuel 7:1).

Eleazar son of Phinehas — weighed out the vessels in the house of Yahuah (Ezra 8:33).

Eleazar the priest — stood with the trumpets at the dedication of the wall (Nehemiah 12:42).

Eleazar the Merarite Levite (1 Chronicles 23:21-22).

Altar, ark, vessels, trumpets. The name is soaked in priesthood. Watch it, because wherever this name surfaces, Yahuah is doing the same thing: **helping a priesthood that cannot help itself**.

**2 · The Line That Survived the Fire**

Aaron had four sons. Two died in the opening days of the priesthood:

**Leviticus 10:1-2**

*...they offered strange fire before the LORD, which he commanded them not. And there went out fire from the LORD, and devoured them, and they died before the LORD.*

Nadab and Abihu are gone. **Eleazar** and **Ithamar** are left standing (Lev 10:6, 12). Every priest for the next thousand years descends from those two brothers — remember that, because the whole story turns on which of the two holds the office. And to Eleazar’s son Phinehas, Yahuah gives a covenant with no expiration:

**Numbers 25:12-13**

*...Behold, I give unto him my covenant of peace: And he shall have it, and his seed after him, even the covenant of an everlasting priesthood.*

**3 · The Garments Pass Through Death**

The first picture of the pattern comes on a mountain. Aaron does not retire — he dies, and the office moves:

**Numbers 20:26, 28**

*And strip Aaron of his garments, and put them upon Eleazar his son... And Moses stripped Aaron of his garments, and put them upon Eleazar his son; and Aaron died there in the top of the mount.*

The garments come off the dying man and go onto Eleazar. The priesthood passes *through* a death and comes out the other side clothed and alive. Hold that word — *clothed*. It returns three more times in this study, and one of those times it tears.

**4 · Twenty-Four Courses**

Generations later David organized the two surviving houses for temple service, and they were not equal:

**1 Chronicles 24:4**

*...there were more chief men found of the sons of Eleazar than of the sons of Ithamar... Among the sons of Eleazar there were sixteen chief men... and eight among the sons of Ithamar.*

Sixteen courses from Eleazar’s house, eight from Ithamar’s — **twenty-four courses** in all (1 Chron 24:7-18), assigned by lot. Alongside them, **twenty-four courses of singers** (1 Chron 25). Twenty-four with the incense, twenty-four with the harps. Hold both numbers; they meet again at the end of the Book.

Each course served in rotation through the year, with all of them gathered for the great feasts. When a course came up, lots were cast for the duties, and the highest honor was to enter the Holy Place and burn incense at the golden altar while the people prayed outside — a lot most priests waited a lifetime for, and many never received.

**5 · The Priesthood Corrupted**

But the high priesthood did not stay in Eleazar’s hands. By Shiloh it sat with **Eli**, of Ithamar’s house — and his sons made the office a stench:

**1 Samuel 2:17**

*Wherefore the sin of the young men was very great before the LORD: for men abhorred the offering of the LORD.*

Yahuah’s sentence on that house is the sentence that governs this entire study:

**1 Samuel 2:35**

*And I will raise me up a faithful priest, that shall do according to that which is in mine heart and in my mind...*

Mark the verb. When a priesthood defiles itself, Yahuah does not reform it, negotiate with it, or wait for it to repent. He **raises up** another. Write that line across the rest of the Bible.

**6 · The Line Raised Back**

The sentence lands in Solomon’s day. Abiathar, last of Eli’s house, is thrust out:

**1 Kings 2:27**

*So Solomon thrust out Abiathar from being priest unto the LORD; that he might fulfil the word of the LORD, which he spake concerning the house of Eli in Shiloh.*

And in his place stands **Zadok** — whose genealogy runs straight back through Phinehas to Eleazar (1 Chron 6:4-8, 50-53). “And Zadok the priest did the king put in the room of Abiathar” (1 Kings 2:35).

Read what just happened. Ithamar’s line held the office and corrupted it. Eleazar’s line had lost the office — and Yahuah **raised it back**. The covenant given to Phinehas held. God has helped. Ezekiel’s temple vision then narrows the priesthood to that same faithful remnant:

**Ezekiel 44:15**

*But the priests the Levites, the sons of Zadok, that kept the charge of my sanctuary when the children of Israel went astray from me, they shall come near to me to minister unto me...*

**7 · The Announcement Inside the Old Order**

Now open the New Testament. The first priest we meet is introduced by his course:

**Luke 1:5**

*...a certain priest named Zacharias, of the course of Abia...*

Zacharias is a Levitical priest, serving the eighth of the twenty-four courses (1 Chron 24:10). His lot that day was to enter the Holy Place and burn incense while the people prayed outside. And there, at the altar, an angel appears and announces a birth:

**Luke 1:13, 17**

*...thy wife Elisabeth shall bear thee a son, and thou shalt call his name John... And he shall go before him in the spirit and power of Elias, to turn the hearts of the fathers to the children...*

A forerunner — an Elijah to prepare a people. Because Zacharias does not believe, he is struck **silent**, unable to speak until the child is named (Luke 1:20, 64). He comes out of the Holy Place unable to pronounce the blessing the people were waiting for.

Sit with that picture. The last Levitical priest we meet at the incense altar is struck *speechless*, and the next voice Scripture raises is his son, grown, crying in the wilderness (Luke 3:2-4). The old order falls quiet at the altar; the word moves outside the walls.

**8 · The Sons of Zadok and the Faithful Priest**

Before we go further we have to settle a question that hangs over everything said so far, because Zadok is not finished when Solomon installs him. Centuries later Ezekiel sees a temple, and standing in it are **the sons of Zadok**:

**Ezekiel 44:15**

*But the priests the Levites, the sons of Zadok, that kept the charge of my sanctuary when the children of Israel went astray from me, they shall come near to me to minister unto me...*

Now, some read Ezekiel 40–48 as the blueprint for a future thousand-year kingdom in which the *entire* sacrificial system is restored exactly as before — as though the blood of Yahushua changed nothing at the altar. That reading cannot stand. Daniel says the Messiah, in the midst of the week, causes *the sacrifice and the oblation to cease* (Dan 9:27) — the sin and atonement offerings that pointed to His blood are **finished**, because the One they pointed to has come:

**Hebrews 10:18**

*Now where remission of these is, there is no more offering for sin.*

So whatever Ezekiel’s temple is, it is not a reopening of the atonement He completed. The offerings that **foreshadowed the cross are done**; His one offering perfected forever those He set apart (Heb 10:12, 14). That door does not open again.

But do not miss what Ezekiel is really pointing at, because the **name** is the key. **Zadok** is not just a man — it is the Hebrew *tsedeq*, *righteousness*. The sons of Zadok are, by name, the sons of righteousness. And there is one other priest in Scripture whose name is built from that same word:

**Hebrews 7:1-2**

*...Melchisedec, king of Salem, priest of the most high God... first being by interpretation King of righteousness...*

Melchi-*zedek* — **king of righteousness**. The *same root* as the sons of *Zadok*. This is the bridge the whole study has been walking toward: Ezekiel’s **sons of righteousness** who keep the charge when all others go astray are pointing straight at the **King of Righteousness** — the Melchizedek priest. Zadok was always the shadow; Melchizedek is the substance; and they are the *same word*.

So look at what actually marks the sons of Zadok in the verse: they *kept the charge when Israel went astray*. That is not a genealogy note; it is a **character** note — righteousness holding fast while the sanctuary is defiled. The whole study has been saying it: when a priesthood corrupts itself, Yahuah raises up one that is faithful.

The sons of Zadok are that faithful priesthood in shadow; the substance is the King of Righteousness who was faithful where all others failed — and the priesthood He raises with Him:

**Hebrews 2:17**

*...that he might be a merciful and faithful high priest in things pertaining to God, to make reconciliation for the sins of the people.*

Ezekiel 44 is not a rival to Hebrews 7. Righteousness (Zadok) points to the King of Righteousness (Melchizedek). It is the same thesis in prophetic dress: when the many go astray, the faithful priest keeps the charge — and *he alone comes near*.

**9 · Neither Altar Nor Seat**

So who is holding the priesthood when Yahushua walks into Jerusalem? The answer takes two parties, because by now the religion of Israel had split in two — and **neither half** was the faithful priesthood.

The **altar** was still Levitical. The priests who ran the temple were the **Sadducees** — the priestly aristocracy, and the high priest came from among them (Acts 4:1; 5:17; 23:6-8 show the priestly leadership is the Sadducee party). Caiaphas was a Sadducee. So the bloodline was right. But the *office* had been corrupted into a political prize: Rome and the Herods appointed and removed high priests at will, and the seat circulated among a few wealthy families. Levitical blood, but a **bought office** — installed by pagan hands, not held by faithful succession.

And here is the irony buried in the name. **Sadducee** (Tseduqim) is built from the same word — *Zadok*, *tsedeq*, *righteousness*. They wore the name of the *sons of Zadok* — the righteous priesthood that kept the charge when Israel went astray — while running the very altar their namesakes were meant to guard faithfully. The name judged them.

The **teaching seat** was held by the other party. The **Pharisees** were *not priests at all* — a lay movement that rose in the centuries after the return from Babylon. They ran the synagogues and held the loyalty of the crowds, but they had no altar. Paul was a Pharisee, and Paul was of **Benjamin** (Rom 11:1; Phil 3:5), not Levi — a man could be a Pharisee without one drop of priestly blood. What they claimed was not Aaron’s altar but Moses’ chair:

**Matthew 23:2**

*The scribes and the Pharisees sit in Moses' seat.*

Their very name says what they did: **Pharisee** comes from *parash* (H6567), *to separate*. They named themselves the separated ones — but separation is Yahuah’s to give, not man’s to take. And what they carried back from the exile was not Torah alone, but Torah *plus* — the tradition of the elders, later written down and still bearing Babylon’s name. Yahushua did not treat it as harmless:

**Mark 7:9**

*...Full well ye reject the commandment of God, that ye may keep your own tradition.*

So the picture at the trial is complete. The **altar was bought** — Levitical priests corrupted into a Roman-appointed aristocracy wearing the name of the righteous. The **seat was seized** — a lay party ruling from a chair it was never given. One priesthood, corrupted at the altar; one lay party, seizing the seat. Neither was the priesthood of Yahuah, and the faithful priest was still owed.

**10 · The Beggar at the Gate**

And to these very men — the robed party with no altar — Yahushua told a parable. Luke names the audience before He begins:

**Luke 16:14**

*And the Pharisees also, who were covetous, heard all these things: and they derided him.*

Then watch how He dresses the rich man, and watch who He puts at his gate:

**Luke 16:19-20**

*There was a certain rich man, which was clothed in purple and fine linen, and fared sumptuously every day: And there was a certain beggar named Lazarus, which was laid at his gate, full of sores...*

Purple and fine linen. Those are the fabrics of the sanctuary — the very materials commanded for the priestly garments: “gold, and blue, and *purple*, and scarlet, and *fine linen*” (Ex 28:5-6). Yahushua paints a man wearing the colors of the holy garments, faring sumptuously, with a starving, sore-covered man at his gate — and he does nothing. The clothing thread again: the robe does not make the priest.

And the beggar’s name? **Lazarus**. **Eleazar**. Out of every character in every parable Yahushua ever told, this is the only one He names — and the name He chose was *God has helped*. No man in purple helped him. Yahuah did.

Then comes the answer from Abraham — and remember who is listening:

**Luke 16:29**

*Abraham saith unto him, They have Moses and the prophets; let them hear them.*

He says *hear Moses* — to the men who sit in *Moses’ seat* and will not hear him. They kept the chair and lost the words. And the parable’s last line is a prophecy:

**Luke 16:31**

*...If they hear not Moses and the prophets, neither will they be persuaded, though one rose from the dead.*

Hold that sentence. It is about to happen literally, to a man with that very name.

**11 · Death Comes First**

When Yahushua turns back toward Judea — where they had just tried to stone Him — the disciples know exactly what road this is:

**John 11:16**

*Then said Thomas, which is called Didymus, unto his fellowdisciples, Let us also go, that we may die with him.*

A priesthood is never raised where nothing has died. The pattern has been fixed since Mount Hor: Aaron dies before Eleazar wears the garments. Now the road to the raising runs through death again, and even the disciples say it out loud before they arrive.

And where are they going? **Bethany** — a name carrying the sense of *house of affliction*, and by another reading, house of the poor and lowly. Not the temple courts. Not the high priest’s palace. The new priesthood does not rise from the seat of power; it rises from the house of affliction. That is the ground Yahuah has always drawn His servants out of — the low place, the grave, the last place anyone would look.

**12 · Eleazar at the Tomb**

Inside the tomb is a man whose name, in Greek, is Lazarus. In Hebrew it is **Eleazar**. The same name as the son of Aaron. The same name as the beggar at the gate. And Yahushua calls it out loud.

**John 11:39**

*...Lord, by this time he stinketh: for he hath been dead four days.*

Four days, not three. The three-day sign belongs to Yahushua alone — His body saw no corruption. Eleazar is carried deliberately past that limit: decay begun, every hope spent, no natural explanation left. That is the whole point of the fourth day. Out of the house of affliction, past all help, the name that means *God has helped* answers when it is called.

**John 11:44**

*And he that was dead came forth, bound hand and foot with graveclothes... Jesus saith unto them, Loose him, and let him go.*

Here the clothing thread closes. A priest is *clothed* into his office — Eleazar into Aaron’s garments on Mount Hor. A false priest is robed in purple while a man starves at his gate. This Eleazar comes out of death still wrapped in the linen of the grave, and the word goes out to strip it off him so he can walk free. A man raised to serve must not stay dressed in the grave.

And now watch the parable come true. Yahushua had said they would not be persuaded though one rose from the dead. One rose from the dead:

**John 12:10-11**

*But the chief priests consulted that they might put Lazarus also to death; Because that by reason of him many... believed on Jesus.*

Not persuaded — provoked. The bought altar tries to **re-bury** the sign. But you do not re-bury what Yahuah has raised. This is not the Messiah’s own resurrection; it is the *sign* of the priesthood He was bringing: called out of death, by name, from the house of affliction.

**13 · The Robe That Tore**

Days later the old order finishes disqualifying itself. Torah gave the high priest one absolute prohibition concerning his garments:

**Leviticus 21:10**

*...the high priest... shall not uncover his head, nor rend his clothes.*

**Matthew 26:65**

*Then the high priest rent his clothes, saying, He hath spoken blasphemy...*

Set the scenes side by side. On Mount Hor the garments are carefully lifted off one priest and placed onto the next — the office honored, transferred, preserved. In Caiaphas’ hall the garments are **torn** by the man wearing them, in the one act his office forbids, in the presence of the true High Priest. The Aaronic order rends its own robe and steps out of its place, exactly as Eli’s house did. And the same word answers it: *I will raise me up a faithful priest*.

**14 · Changed, Not Repaired**

This time the answer is not another son of Aaron. Zadok replaced Abiathar and the office stayed Aaronic — a repair within the family. What happens now goes deeper than the family:

**Hebrews 7:11-12**

*If therefore perfection were by the Levitical priesthood... what further need was there that another priest should rise after the order of Melchisedec, and not be called after the order of Aaron? For the priesthood being changed, there is made of necessity a change also of the law.*

Yahushua does not replace Caiaphas within the old system. He is High Priest after the order of **Melchizedek** — an order Aaron’s house could only point toward. The courses had done their work: they kept the incense burning until the One they pointed to walked into the temple.

And the priesthood raised at Bethany? Not Aaron’s — that line is being replaced. It is **ours**:

**1 Peter 2:9**

*But ye are a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, an holy nation, a peculiar people...*

**Revelation 1:6**

*And hath made us kings and priests unto God and his Father...*

**15 · What Law Was Changed?**

Now we have to stop and deal with what modern Christianity has done to that verse. “The priesthood being changed, there is made of necessity a change also of the law” — Hebrews 7:12 is lifted out of a priesthood chapter and swung at Torah as though Yahuah had repealed His own commandments. Read the chapter it lives in and it cannot mean that.

Start with the word. “Change” is **metathesis**, from *metatithemi* — to **transfer**, to move a thing from one place to another. It is the same word the same letter uses of Enoch:

**Hebrews 11:5**

*By faith Enoch was translated that he should not see death; and was not found, because God had translated him...*

Enoch was not abolished. Enoch was *moved*. That is the verb. The priesthood is transferred, and of necessity the law governing it is transferred with it. Nothing in the word means annihilation.

Transferred from what, and why? The chapter answers plainly four verses later:

**Hebrews 7:13-14**

*For he of whom these things are spoken pertaineth to another tribe, of which no man gave attendance at the altar. For it is evident that our Lord sprang out of Juda; of which tribe Moses spake nothing concerning priesthood.*

There is the entire problem the chapter exists to solve. Torah assigned the priesthood to **Levi**. Yahushua came from **Judah**. If the priesthood moves to Him, then the law that assigns priests by tribe must move with it — because a priest out of Judah cannot stand under a statute that says priests come out of Levi. That is the law being transferred. Not the Sabbath. Not the feasts. Not the commandments. The law of **priestly descent**.

And the writer says so again, in words the pulpit reads right past:

**Hebrews 7:16**

*Who is made, not after the law of a carnal commandment, but after the power of an endless life.*

A *carnal* commandment — a fleshly one. What was fleshly about the Levitical priesthood? Its qualification was **ancestry**. Flesh and blood decided who stood at the altar. Yahushua qualifies by no genealogy at all, but by an endless life. The “carnal commandment” is the rule of descent, and nothing else in the chapter is in view.

Even where the letter does speak of setting something aside, count the nouns:

**Hebrews 7:18**

*For there is verily a disannulling of the commandment going before for the weakness and unprofitableness thereof.*

The commandment — **singular**. One *entolē*, one specific commandment going before: the one that said the priest must come from Levi. Not “the commandments.” Not the law entire. One rule, about one office, set aside because the Man it must now describe came from the wrong tribe to fit it.

And if anyone still insists the covenant change means the law is gone, let the letter finish its own sentence. When Hebrews reaches the new covenant, it quotes Jeremiah — and hear what the new covenant actually does with the law:

*...I will put my laws into their mind, and write them in their hearts: and I will be to them a God, and they shall be to me a people.* — Hebrews 8:10 (Jeremiah 31:33)

The law is not deleted in the new covenant. It is **relocated** — off the stone and into the man. Transferred, exactly like the priesthood, exactly like Enoch. The chapter Christianity uses to bury Torah is a chapter about a Priest from the wrong tribe, and the covenant they say abolished the law is the covenant that writes it on the heart.

**16 · Twenty-Four Before the Throne**

Now the number returns. When the scene opens in heaven, the ordered service of the courses is still standing — but changed:

**Revelation 5:8**

*...four and twenty elders fell down before the Lamb, having every one of them harps, and golden vials full of odours, which are the prayers of the saints.*

Harps and incense — the twenty-four courses of singers and the twenty-four courses of priests, together before the throne. The rotation is over. No one waits a lifetime for the lot to fall. And what are they called?

**Revelation 5:10**

*And hast made us unto our God kings and priests: and we shall reign on the earth.*

Kings and priests — the same words spoken over the redeemed. The courses of Eleazar end not in a temple built with hands, but in a raised priesthood before the Lamb, holding the prayers of the saints where the incense used to be.

Follow the whole line and the lesson never changes. The name survives the fire. The garments pass through a death. The office is corrupted and Yahuah *raises up* a faithful priest. The courses turn for a thousand years and announce the forerunner at the incense altar. Then a bought high priest tears the robe he was forbidden to touch while a lay party with no altar rules from a seat it was never given — and Yahuah does not patch that priesthood. He raises another out of death entirely, and transfers the order itself to Melchizedek — moving the law of descent to make room for a Priest out of Judah, and writing His commandments on the hearts of the men He raised.

From Eleazar on Mount Hor, to Zadok in Abiathar’s room, to a beggar named Eleazar at a rich man’s gate, to Eleazar walking out of a tomb still bound in grave-linen, to twenty-four crowns before the throne — the name has been saying the same thing the whole way: *God has helped*.
